The Neumann addition theorem for Bessel functions of the first kind states that for real $r, s > 0$ with $s < r$ and $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$J_0\!\left(\sqrt{r^2 + s^2 - 2rs\cos\phi}\right) = J_0(r)\,J_0(s) + 2\sum_{m=1}^{\infty} J_m(r)\,J_m(s)\,\cos(m\phi),$$

where the series on the right converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of the given parameter domain.

The modified Bessel function of the first kind is related to $J_\nu$ by

$$I_m(z) \;=\; i^{-m}\,J_m(iz), \qquad m \in \mathbb{Z},\;\; z > 0.$$

Claim: For real $r, s > 0$ with $s < r$ and all $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$, the following identity holds:

$$I_0\!\left(\sqrt{r^2 + s^2 - 2rs\cos\phi}\right) = I_0(r)\,I_0(s) + 2\sum_{m=1}^{\infty} I_m(r)\,I_m(s)\,\cos(m\phi).$$

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

The claim is false.

Set $\phi=0$. Then

$$\sqrt{r^2+s^2-2rs\cos0}=\sqrt{(r-s)^2}=r-s,$$

since $r>s>0$. Thus the claimed identity becomes

$$I_0(r-s)=I_0(r)I_0(s)+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty I_m(r)I_m(s).$$

Now use the standard Fourier expansion

$$e^{t\cos\theta}=I_0(t)+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty I_m(t)\cos(m\theta),\qquad t>0.$$

Multiply the expansions for $t=r$ and $t=s$, then integrate over $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$. By orthogonality of the cosine functions,

$$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}e^{r\cos\theta}e^{s\cos\theta}d\theta = I_0(r)I_0(s)+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty I_m(r)I_m(s).$$

Since

$$e^{r\cos\theta}e^{s\cos\theta}=e^{(r+s)\cos\theta},$$

the right-hand side equals

$$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}e^{(r+s)\cos\theta}d\theta=I_0(r+s).$$

Hence

$$I_0(r)I_0(s)+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty I_m(r)I_m(s)=I_0(r+s).$$

Therefore the claim would force

$$I_0(r-s)=I_0(r+s).$$

But $I_0$ is strictly increasing on $(0,\infty)$ because

$$I_0^\prime(x)=I_1(x)>0\qquad(x>0).$$

Since $r+s>r-s\ge0$, it follows that

$$I_0(r+s)>I_0(r-s),$$

which contradicts the claimed identity. Therefore the claim is false.


The correct identity is

$$I_0\left(\sqrt{r^2+s^2-2rs\cos\phi}\right)=I_0(r)I_0(s)+2\sum_{m=1}^\infty(-1)^mI_m(r)I_m(s)\cos(m\phi),$$

which follows directly from the Neumann addition theorem under the substitution $r\mapsto ir$, $s\mapsto is$, together with $I_m(z)=i^{-m}J_m(iz)$.
