For integer $n\ge0$, the spherical Bessel functions $j_n(z)$ are defined recursively by

$$j_0(z)=\frac{\sin z}{z}, \qquad j_1(z)=\frac{\sin z}{z^2}-\frac{\cos z}{z},$$

and for $n\ge1$,

$$j_{n+1}(z)=\frac{2n+1}{z}j_n(z)-j_{n-1}(z).$$

Claim: For every integer $n\ge0$, there exist unique polynomials $p_n$ and $q_n$ with real coefficients such that $q_0\equiv0$, $\deg p_n=n$, $\deg q_n=n-1$ for $n\ge1$, and

$$j_n(z)=\frac{p_n(1/z)\sin z+q_n(1/z)\cos z}{z}$$

for all $z\ne0$.

Determine whether this claim is true or false, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

The claim is true.

Existence:

For $n=0$, $j_0(z)=\frac{\sin z}{z}$, so take $p_0(x)=1$, $q_0(x)=0$.

For $n=1$,

$$j_1(z)=\frac{\sin z}{z^2}-\frac{\cos z}{z}=\frac{(1/z)\sin z+(-1)\cos z}{z},$$

so take $p_1(x)=x$, $q_1(x)=-1$.

Assume for some $n\ge1$ that

$$j_n(z)=\frac{p_n(1/z)\sin z+q_n(1/z)\cos z}{z},$$

$$j_{n-1}(z)=\frac{p_{n-1}(1/z)\sin z+q_{n-1}(1/z)\cos z}{z}.$$

Substitute into the recurrence:

$$j_{n+1}(z)=\frac{2n+1}{z}j_n(z)-j_{n-1}(z).$$

Let $x=1/z$. Then

$$j_{n+1}(z)=\frac{\bigl((2n+1)x p_n(x)-p_{n-1}(x)\bigr)\sin z+\bigl((2n+1)x q_n(x)-q_{n-1}(x)\bigr)\cos z}{z}.$$

Define

$$p_{n+1}(x)=(2n+1)x p_n(x)-p_{n-1}(x),$$

$$q_{n+1}(x)=(2n+1)x q_n(x)-q_{n-1}(x).$$

These are polynomials, so the representation holds for $n+1$.

Degree:

$\deg p_{n+1}=n+1$ since $\deg(xp_n)=n+1$ and $\deg p_{n-1}=n-1$.

For $q$, one checks

$$q_2(x)=3xq_1(x)-q_0(x)=-3x,$$

so $\deg q_2=1$. For $n\ge2$, $\deg(xq_n)=n$ and $\deg q_{n-1}=n-2$, hence $\deg q_{n+1}=n$.

Thus existence with correct degrees follows by induction.

Uniqueness:

Suppose

$$\frac{P(1/z)\sin z+Q(1/z)\cos z}{z}\equiv0.$$

Then

$$P(1/z)\sin z+Q(1/z)\cos z\equiv0.$$

Take $z=k\pi$ with $k\ne0$. Then $\sin z=0$ and $\cos z=(-1)^k$, so

$$Q\left(\frac1{k\pi}\right)=0.$$

Since this holds for infinitely many zeros, the polynomial $Q$ must be identically zero.

Then $P(1/z)\sin z\equiv0$. Taking $z=(m+\tfrac12)\pi$ gives $\sin z=\pm1$, so

$$P\left(\frac1{(m+\tfrac12)\pi}\right)=0$$

for infinitely many values accumulating at 0, hence $P\equiv0$.

Therefore the representation polynomials are unique.
