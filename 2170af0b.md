Let $Y_{\ell m}(\theta, \phi)$ be the standard orthonormal spherical harmonics on $S^2$. We define the kernel $K_N$ between the north pole $\Omega_1 = (0,0)$ and an interior point $\Omega_2 = (\theta_0, 0)$ as:

$$K_N(\theta_0) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{N} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell} Y_{\ell m}(0,0) Y_{\ell m}^*(\theta_0, 0)$$

Let $S_N(\theta_0)$ be the normalized partial sum defined by:

$$S_N(\theta_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} K_N(\theta_0)$$

Claim: For any fixed $\theta_0 \in (0, \pi)$, the sequence $\{S_N(\theta_0)\}_{N=1}^{\infty}$ is bounded as $N \to \infty$.

Determine with rigorous proof whether the claim is True or False.

Conclusion: True.

At the north pole, $Y_{\ell m}(0,0)=0$ for all $m\ne0$, while

$$Y_{\ell0}(0,0)=\sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi}}.$$

Therefore, the kernel reduces to the zonal sum:

$$K_{N}(\theta_{0})=\frac1{4\pi}\sum_{\ell=0}^{N}(2\ell+1)P_{\ell}(\cos\theta_{0}).$$

The Christoffelâ€“Darboux identity provides:

$$\sum_{\ell=0}^{N}(2\ell+1)P_{\ell}(x)=\frac{N+1}{1-x}\bigl(P_{N}(x)-P_{N+1}(x)\bigr).$$

Substituting $x=\cos\theta_{0}$:

$$K_{N}(\theta_{0})=\frac{N+1}{4\pi(1-\cos\theta_{0})}\bigl(P_{N}(\cos\theta_{0})-P_{N+1}(\cos\theta_{0})\bigr).$$

For fixed $\theta_{0}\in(0,\pi)$, the interior asymptotic for Legendre polynomials is:

$$P_{N}(\cos\theta_{0})=A(\theta_{0})N^{-1/2}\cos\left((N+\tfrac12)\theta_{0}-\tfrac\pi4\right)+O(N^{-3/2}), \quad A(\theta_{0})=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi\sin\theta_{0}}}.$$

When calculating the difference $P_N - P_{N+1}$, the amplitude $(N+1)^{-1/2}$ is expanded as $N^{-1/2} + O(N^{-3/2})$. Using the cosine subtraction identity:

$$P_{N}(\cos\theta_{0})-P_{N+1}(\cos\theta_{0}) = 2A(\theta_{0})N^{-1/2}\sin\left((N+1)\theta_{0}-\tfrac\pi4\right)\sin\left(\tfrac{\theta_{0}}2\right)+O(N^{-3/2}).$$

Since $\theta_0$ is fixed, the factor $\sin(\theta_0/2)$ is a positive constant. This confirms the difference is $O(N^{-1/2})$.

Substituting the difference back into the kernel formula:

$$K_{N}(\theta_{0}) = \frac{N+1}{4\pi(1-\cos\theta_0)} \cdot O(N^{-1/2}) = O(\sqrt N).$$

Consequently:

$$S_{N}(\theta_{0})=\frac{K_{N}(\theta_0)}{\sqrt N} = \frac{O(\sqrt N)}{\sqrt N} = O(1).$$

The sequence $\{S_{N}(\theta_{0})\}$ is bounded as $N\to\infty$.

Conclusion: True.
