Let $\{p_n(x)\}_{n=0}^\infty$ be the sequence of orthonormal polynomials with respect to a positive weight $w(x)$ on an interval $[a,b]$, satisfying the three-term recurrence

$$x p_n(x)=a_{n+1}p_{n+1}(x)+b_n p_n(x)+a_n p_{n-1}(x)\qquad (a_{n+1}>0).$$

For a fixed integer $N\ge0$ and a fixed $x_0\in(a,b)$, define the one-parameter family of polynomials

$$\pi_t(x)=p_{N+1}(x)-t p_N(x),\qquad t\in\mathbb{R}.$$

Prove or disprove the following recurrence relation with full mathematical rigor:

$$\inf_{t\in\mathbb{R}}\frac{\|\pi_t\|_w^2}{\pi_t(x_0)^2} < \inf_{\substack{Q\in\mathbb{P}_{N+1}\\Q(x_0)\ne0}}\frac{\|Q\|_w^2}{Q(x_0)^2}.$$


The proposed extremal inequality is false.

From orthonormality one has

$$\|p_{N+1}-t p_N\|_w^2=\|p_{N+1}\|_w^2-2t\langle p_{N+1},p_N\rangle_w+t^2\|p_N\|_w^2=1+t^2.$$

Let $A=p_N(x_0)$ and $B=p_{N+1}(x_0)$. Then

$$\pi_t(x_0)=B-tA$$

and

$$F(t)=\frac{1+t^2}{(B-tA)^2}.$$

We analyze the infimum across all possible cases for $A$ and $B$. By the interlacing theorem for zeros of consecutive orthogonal polynomials (Szegő, Theorem 3.3.2, Orthogonal Polynomials, 4th ed., 1975), $p_N$ and $p_{N+1}$ cannot both vanish at the same interior point $x_0$. Thus $A^2+B^2>0$.

Case 1: $A\neq0$ and $B\neq0$

In the general case $A\neq0$ and $B\neq0$, the function $F(t)=(1+t^2)/(B-tA)^2$ is smooth on $\mathbb{R}$, and its derivative vanishes only when the numerator and denominator gradients balance. Setting $F^\prime(t)=0$ reduces to a linear equation in $t$, whose unique solution is $t^*=-A/B$. Substituting gives

$$F(t^*)=\frac{1}{A^2+B^2}=\frac{1}{p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2}.$$

As $|t|\to\infty$, $F(t)\sim1/A^2>1/(A^2+B^2)$, so $t^*$ is the global minimiser.

Case 2: $A=0$

Then $B\neq0$, and

$$F(t)=\frac{1+t^2}{B^2}.$$

Minimized at $t^*=0$, with

$$\inf F(t)=\frac{1}{B^2}=\frac{1}{A^2+B^2}.$$

Case 3: $B=0$

Then $A\neq0$, and

$$F(t)=\frac{1+t^2}{t^2A^2}=\frac{1}{A^2}+\frac{1}{t^2A^2}.$$

Thus

$$\inf F(t)=\frac{1}{A^2}=\frac{1}{A^2+B^2}.$$

Therefore, in all cases,

$$\inf_{t\in\mathbb R}\frac{\|p_{N+1}-t p_N\|_w^2}{(p_{N+1}(x_0)-t p_N(x_0))^2}=\frac{1}{p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2}.$$

For the full space $\mathbb{P}_{N+1}$, any $Q$ has expansion

$$Q=\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}c_k p_k.$$

Then

$$\|Q\|_w^2=\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}c_k^2,\qquad Q(x_0)=\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}c_k p_k(x_0).$$

Cauchy–Schwarz gives

$$Q(x_0)^2\le\Bigl(\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}c_k^2\Bigr)\Bigl(\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}p_k(x_0)^2\Bigr),$$

with equality only for $c_k\propto p_k(x_0)$. Thus

$$\inf_{\substack{Q\in\mathbb P_{N+1}\\Q(x_0)\ne0}}\frac{\|Q\|_w^2}{Q(x_0)^2}=\frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}p_k(x_0)^2}.$$

Since $p_0(x_0)\neq0$,

$$\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}p_k(x_0)^2=\left(\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}p_k(x_0)^2\right)+p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2>p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2,$$

so

$$\frac{1}{p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2}>\frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}p_k(x_0)^2}.$$

When $N=0$,

$$\frac{1}{p_0(x_0)^2+p_1(x_0)^2}=\frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^1 p_k(x_0)^2},$$

so the two infima are equal.

Conclusion. For all $N\ge0$,

$$\inf_{t\in\mathbb R}\frac{\|\pi_t\|_w^2}{\pi_t(x_0)^2}=\frac{1}{p_N(x_0)^2+p_{N+1}(x_0)^2}\ge\frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^{N+1}p_k(x_0)^2},$$

with strict inequality for $N\ge1$.

Thus the proposed inequality

$$\inf_{t\in\mathbb R}\frac{\|\pi_t\|_w^2}{\pi_t(x_0)^2}<\inf_{\substack{Q\in\mathbb P_{N+1}\\Q(x_0)\ne0}}\frac{\|Q\|_w^2}{Q(x_0)^2}$$

is false in general.
