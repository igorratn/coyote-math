For integer $n \ge 0$, the Bessel function $J_n(z)$ is given by $J_n(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \cos(n\theta - z \sin \theta) \, d\theta$. 

For non-integer $\nu > 0$, define $F_\nu(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \cos(\nu\theta - z \sin \theta) \, d\theta$.

 Define $J_\nu(z)$ for non-integer $\nu$ by the power series $J_\nu(z) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{m! \Gamma(m + \nu + 1)} \left( \frac{z}{2} \right)^{2m + \nu}$.

Claim: $F_\nu(z) = J_\nu(z)$ for all non-integer $\nu > 0$ and all $z > 0$. 

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

The claim is False.

Fix a non-integer $\nu > 0$ with $\sin(2\pi\nu) \neq 0$ (for example, $\nu = 1/4$). For all $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$ and $z \ge 0$, we have the bound $|\cos(\nu\theta - z \sin \theta)| \le 1$. Since the integrand is uniformly bounded by the integrable function $1$ on $[0,2\pi]$, the Dominated Convergence Theorem justifies interchanging the limit and the integral:

$$\lim_{z \to 0^+} F_\nu(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \lim_{z \to 0^+} \cos(\nu\theta - z \sin \theta) \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \cos(\nu\theta) \, d\theta.$$

Evaluating the integral:

$$\int_0^{2\pi} \cos(\nu\theta) \, d\theta = \left[ \frac{\sin(\nu\theta)}{\nu} \right]_0^{2\pi} = \frac{\sin(2\pi\nu)}{\nu},$$

hence,

$$\lim_{z \to 0^+} F_\nu(z) = \frac{\sin(2\pi\nu)}{2\pi\nu} \neq 0.$$

On the other hand, from the defining power series of $J_\nu$:

$$J_\nu(z) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\nu+1)} \left( \frac{z}{2} \right)^\nu + O(z^{\nu+2}) \quad \text{as} \quad z \to 0^+.$$

For every $\nu > 0$, the presence of the $z^\nu$ term ensures that:

$$\lim_{z \to 0^+} J_\nu(z) = 0.$$

Therefore, $F_\nu(z) \neq J_\nu(z)$ for such $\nu$. The claimed identity cannot hold for all non-integer $\nu > 0$ and all $z > 0$.
