Let $A=\{x\in\mathbb{R}: x\not=0 \text{ and } x - \sin(1/x)>0\}$
Determine whether $A$ possesses a greatest lower bound.

For large $|x|$, $\sin(1/x)\approx1/x$, so $x-\sin(1/x)\approx x$. Hence all sufficiently large positive $x$ belong to $A$, while large negative $x$ do not.
For $x$ near $0$, $\sin(1/x)$ oscillates between $-1$ and $1$. Negative points $x_k=-1/(\pi/2+2\pi k)$ satisfy $\sin(1/x_k)=-1$, thus
$$x_k-\sin(1/x_k)=x_k+1>0$$
and $x_k\to0^-$. Therefore $A$ contains negative numbers arbitrarily close to $0$.
The boundary of $A$ is determined by $x=\sin(1/x)$. On $(-1,0)$ this equation has a smallest root $\alpha$ because
$$(-1)-\sin(-1)<0,\quad(-2/\pi)-\sin(-\pi/2)>0$$
so by continuity $\alpha\in(-1,-2/\pi)$. Numerically $\alpha\approx-0.89754$.
Hence $A$ possesses a greatest lower bound
$$\inf A=\alpha\approx-0.89754$$
where $\alpha$ is the smallest solution of $x=\sin(1/x)$ in $(-1,0)$.
