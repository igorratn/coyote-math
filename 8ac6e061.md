For $a, b, c > 0$ satisfying the strict triangle inequality ($a < b + c$, $b < a + c$, $c < a + b$), define the Bessel triangle functional

$$\mathcal{B}(a, b, c) = 2\pi\, A(a,b,c) \int_0^{\infty} J_0(at)\, J_0(bt)\, J_0(ct)\, dt,$$

where $J_0$ denotes the Bessel function of the first kind of order zero and $A(a,b,c)$ is the area of the triangle with side lengths $a, b, c$, given by Heron's formula:

$$A = \frac{1}{4}\sqrt{2(a^2 b^2 + b^2 c^2 + c^2 a^2) - (a^4 + b^4 + c^4)}.$$

The integral converges absolutely when the triangle inequality holds and arises in potential theory and lattice Green's function computations. The prefactor $2\pi A$ is the natural normalization from the geometry of the triangle.

Claim: For all $a, b, c > 0$ satisfying the strict triangle inequality,

$$\mathcal{B}(a, b, c) = 1.$$

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

---

## Solution

The claim is False. $\mathcal{B}(a,b,c)$ is not a constant.

A scaling argument immediately disproves the claim. Under the substitution $a \to \lambda a$, $b \to \lambda b$, $c \to \lambda c$ for $\lambda > 0$:
- The area scales as $A(\lambda a, \lambda b, \lambda c) = \lambda^2 A(a,b,c)$.
- By the change of variable $u = \lambda t$ in the integral: $\int_0^\infty J_0(\lambda a t) J_0(\lambda b t) J_0(\lambda c t)\, dt = \frac{1}{\lambda}\int_0^\infty J_0(au) J_0(bu) J_0(cu)\, du$.

Therefore $\mathcal{B}(\lambda a, \lambda b, \lambda c) = 2\pi \cdot \lambda^2 A \cdot \frac{1}{\lambda} \int J_0^3\, du = \lambda \cdot \mathcal{B}(a,b,c)$.

Since $\mathcal{B}$ scales as $\lambda$, it cannot equal a constant (such as $1$) for all valid triangles. Explicitly, at $a = b = c = 1$ vs $a = b = c = 2$, $\mathcal{B}$ differs by a factor of $2$.

Note: the well-known triple Bessel identity $\int_0^\infty J_0(at) J_0(bt) J_0(ct)\, t\, dt = 1/(2\pi A)$ uses the weighted measure $t\, dt$, which gives a different scaling ($\lambda^{-2}$ instead of $\lambda^{-1}$) that exactly cancels the $\lambda^2$ from $A$, producing a constant. The problem's integral uses the unweighted measure $dt$, breaking this cancellation. $\blacksquare$
