## STEP 1 — Prompt

A smooth oriented surface $S$ has local coordinates $(u,v)$ in which the area element is $dA = du\,dv$ and the Gaussian curvature is $K(u) = 3e^{-u^2}$. A tangent vector is parallel-transported once around the boundary of the triangular region with vertices $(0, 0)$, $(0.800, 0)$, and $(0.800, 1.00)$, traversed counterclockwise in the $(u,v)$-plane. Let $\Delta\theta$ denote the signed angle from the initial vector to the returned vector, taken positive for counterclockwise rotation in the tangent plane at the starting point. Compute $\Delta\theta$ in $\text{rad}$ and report your answer as a decimal rounded to three significant figures.

## STEP 5 — Failure Rationale Summary

The dominant failure across all responses is the sign convention in the holonomy formula. When a tangent vector is parallel-transported counterclockwise around a loop bounding a region with positive integrated curvature, the Gauss-Bonnet theorem dictates that the accumulated rotation is

$$\Delta\theta = -\iint_T K\,dA.$$

Models consistently applied $+\iint_T K\,dA$, obtaining $+0.886\text{ rad}$ instead of $-0.886\text{ rad}$. This reflects a genuine conceptual misunderstanding of the orientation convention: a counterclockwise traversal on a positively curved region causes the transported vector to rotate clockwise (negatively) relative to the initial direction. A secondary failure mode involved misidentifying the triangular integration region as a rectangle, leading to an incorrect magnitude as well.

## STEP 6 — Step-by-Step Solution + Final Answer

**Steps:**

**Step 1:** For parallel transport once counterclockwise around a simple closed curve bounding a region $T$ on an oriented surface, the signed rotation angle is $\Delta\theta = -\iint_T K\,dA$ under the stated convention that counterclockwise rotation at the starting tangent plane is positive.

**Step 2:** The triangle has vertices $(0,0)$, $(0.800,0)$, and $(0.800,1.00)$. The side from $(0,0)$ to $(0.800,1.00)$ has equation $v = \frac{1.00}{0.800}u = 1.25u$. Hence for $0 \le u \le 0.800$, we have $0 \le v \le 1.25u$.

**Step 3:** Therefore

$\iint_T K\,dA = \int_0^{0.800}\int_0^{1.25u} 3e^{-u^2}\,dv\,du$.

Since the integrand does not depend on $v$, this becomes

$\iint_T K\,dA = \int_0^{0.800} 3.75\,u\,e^{-u^2}\,du$.

**Step 4:** Let $w = u^2$, so $dw = 2u\,du$. Then

$\iint_T K\,dA = 3.75 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{0.64} e^{-w}\,dw = 1.875[-e^{-w}]_0^{0.64} = 1.875\left(1 - e^{-0.64}\right)$.

**Step 5:** Numerically, $e^{-0.64} \approx 0.52729$, so

$\iint_T K\,dA \approx 1.875(1 - 0.52729) \approx 0.88633$.

**Step 6:** Applying the sign convention,

$\Delta\theta = -\iint_T K\,dA \approx -0.88633\text{ rad}$.

Rounded to three significant figures, $\Delta\theta = -0.886\text{ rad}$.

**Step 7:** This is consistent with the fact that the curvature integral is dimensionless, so the rotation angle is measured in $\text{rad}$.

**Final Answer:** $-0.886$

**Final Answer Format:** Decimal
