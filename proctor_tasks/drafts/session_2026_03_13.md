## What I Need To Study

### Book: "A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett
Igor wants me to study this book deeply. Key relevance:

**Bennett's Five Breakthroughs:**
1. Steering (600M years ago) — approach/avoid
2. Reinforcement learning (500M years ago) — trial and error, pattern recognition
3. Simulation (150M years ago) — neocortex, world models, imagining outcomes
4. Mentalizing (25M years ago) — theory of mind, predicting others' behavior
5. Language (100K years ago) — symbols, compositionality

**Why this matters for Proctor:**
- Current LLMs have Breakthroughs 1, 2, and 5 (steering, pattern matching, language)
- They're MISSING Breakthroughs 3 and 4 (simulation, mentalizing)
- The gap between pattern recognition (B2) and simulation (B3) is EXACTLY where Proctor problems should target
- Models RECOGNIZE patterns (standard problems) but don't SIMULATE what happens when conditions change
- This is why "slightly changing the setting" trips them — they don't build a world model to test against the new conditions

**Study goals:**
- Understand Breakthrough 3 (simulation) deeply — what does it mean to build a world model?
- Understand the gap between B2 and B3 — how does pattern recognition fail without simulation?
- Apply these insights to problem design: create problems that REQUIRE simulation (world modeling) to solve correctly, not just pattern recognition

### Igor's Key Insight: "Models parrot"
- Models don't really understand problems — they pattern-match to standard types
- If you slightly change the setting from the standard version, models apply the standard solution without noticing the change
- This is a Breakthrough 2 → Breakthrough 3 gap: recognition without simulation
- The stumble guide's 19 trap types are all instances of this gap

### TRIZ Application to Proctor
- The CONTRADICTION is: models can solve any standard problem, but we need them to fail
- RESOLUTION: create problems that LOOK standard but have a changed setting that alters the answer
- The changed setting must be SUBTLE enough that pattern recognition misses it
- But CONSEQUENTIAL enough that the answer changes significantly (>10%)
- The physics/math must be NATURAL — the trap emerges from the structure, not from tricks
