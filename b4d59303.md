Let $L_n^{(\alpha)}(t)$ be the Laguerre polynomials with $\mathrm{Re}(\alpha)>-1$. For $z\in\mathbb{C}\setminus[0,\infty)$, define the Laguerre function of the second kind:

$$Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z)=\int_0^\infty\frac{t^\alpha e^{-t}L_n^{(\alpha)}(t)}{z-t}dt.$$

Claim: For every $n\ge1$, the limit $\lim\limits_{z\to\infty}z^{n+1}Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z)$ exists and equals $(-1)^{n}\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)$.

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

Conclusion: True.

Fix $\text{Re}(\alpha)>-1$ and $n\ge1$. For $z\in\mathbb C\setminus[0,\infty)$ define

$$Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z)=\int_0^\infty\frac{t^\alpha e^{-t}L_n^{(\alpha)}(t)}{z-t}dt,$$

with the principal branch of $t^\alpha$.

Nikiforov and Uvarov define the function of the second kind (A. F. Nikiforov and V. B. Uvarov, Special Functions of Mathematical Physics, Birkhäuser, 1988, Chapter II, §11) by

$$Q_n^{NU}(z)=\frac{1}{\rho(z)}\int_a^b\frac{\rho(t)y_n(t)}{t-z}dt,$$

and prove the asymptotic expansion (Eq. 7)

$$Q_n^{NU}(z)\sim-\frac{d_n^2}{a_n \rho(z) z^{n+1}} \quad (z\to\infty, z\notin[a,b]),$$

where $a_n$ is the leading coefficient of $y_n$ and $d_n^2=\int_a^b\rho(t)y_n(t)^2dt$.

In the Laguerre case $(a,b)=(0,\infty)$, $y_n(t)=L_n^{(\alpha)}(t)$ and $\rho(t)=t^\alpha e^{-t}$. Since $(z-t)^{-1}=-(t-z)^{-1}$,

$$Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z)=-\rho(z)Q_n^{NU}(z), \quad \rho(z)=z^\alpha e^{-z}.$$

Multiplying the NU asymptotic by $-\rho(z)$ gives

$$Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z)\sim\frac{d_n^2}{a_n} z^{-(n+1)}.$$

For Laguerre polynomials, $a_n=(-1)^n/n!$ and $d_n^2=\Gamma(n+\alpha+1)/n!$, hence

$$\lim_{z\to\infty} z^{n+1}Q_n^{(\alpha)}(z) = \frac{d_n^2}{a_n} = (-1)^n\Gamma(n+\alpha+1).$$

Thus the claim holds.
