For $0 < x < 1$, the Kapteyn series identity states that

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} J_n(nx) = \frac{x}{2(1-x)},$$

where $J_n$ denotes the Bessel function of the first kind of integer order $n$. This identity, due to Kapteyn (1893), converges absolutely for $0 < x < 1$.

For $0 < x < 1$, the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} J_n(nx)/n$ also converges absolutely. Define the **Kapteyn exponential** by

$$\Phi_K(x) = \exp\!\left(2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{J_n(nx)}{n}\right).$$

Claim: For all $0 < x < 1$,

$$\Phi_K(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}.$$

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.


The claim is False.

The claim $\Phi_K(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}$ is equivalent to $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{J_n(nx)}{n} = -\frac{1}{2}\ln(1-x)$, since $\exp\!\bigl(2 \cdot (-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1-x))\bigr) = \frac{1}{1-x}$. Both sides define analytic functions on $(0,1)$, so if the identity held, their Taylor expansions at $x = 0$ would agree. We show they do not.

Using the standard expansion $J_n(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{k!(n+k)!}\bigl(\frac{z}{2}\bigr)^{n+2k}$, we compute the $x^3$ coefficient of $\sum \frac{J_n(nx)}{n}$. Since $\frac{J_n(nx)}{n} = O(x^n)$, only $n = 1$ and $n = 3$ contribute at order $x^3$:

- $n = 1$: $\frac{J_1(x)}{1} = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^3}{16} + O(x^5)$. Coefficient of $x^3$: $-\frac{1}{16}$.
- $n = 3$: $\frac{J_3(3x)}{3} = \frac{(3x)^3}{48 \cdot 3} + O(x^5) = \frac{3x^3}{16} + O(x^5)$. Coefficient of $x^3$: $\frac{3}{16}$.
- $n = 2$: $\frac{J_2(2x)}{2} = \frac{x^2}{4} + O(x^4)$. No $x^3$ term.
- $n \geq 4$: $\frac{J_n(nx)}{n} = O(x^n) = O(x^4)$. No $x^3$ term.

Total $x^3$ coefficient: $-\frac{1}{16} + \frac{3}{16} = \frac{1}{8}$.

But $-\frac{1}{2}\ln(1-x) = \frac{x}{2} + \frac{x^2}{4} + \frac{x^3}{6} + \cdots$ has $x^3$ coefficient $\frac{1}{6}$.

Since $\frac{1}{8} \neq \frac{1}{6}$, the identity $\sum \frac{J_n(nx)}{n} = -\frac{1}{2}\ln(1-x)$ is false, and therefore $\Phi_K(x) \neq \frac{1}{1-x}$.
