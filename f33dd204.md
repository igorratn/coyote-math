
For $\nu > -1/2$, the Bessel function of the first kind $J_\nu(z)$ admits the Poisson integral representation:

$$J_\nu(z) = \frac{(z/2)^\nu}{\sqrt{\pi}\Gamma(\nu+1/2)} \int_0^\pi \cos(z \cos \theta) \sin^{2\nu} \theta \, d\theta$$

Separately, $J_\nu(z)$ is defined for all $\nu \in \mathbb{C}$ by the power series:

$$J_\nu(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{k! \Gamma(k+\nu+1)} \left(\frac{z}{2}\right)^{2k+\nu}$$

Claim: The Poisson integral representation extends by analytic continuation of the parameter $\nu$ to all $\nu$ with $\operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1$, yielding $J_\nu(z)$ on this larger half-plane.

----------

The claim is True.

Fix $z \in \mathbb{C}$ and interpret $(z/2)^\nu = \exp(\nu \log(z/2))$ with a fixed branch of the logarithm. For $\operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1/2$, set:

$$F(\nu) = \frac{(z/2)^\nu}{\sqrt{\pi}\Gamma(\nu+1/2)} \int_0^\pi \cos(z \cos \theta) \sin^{2\nu} \theta \, d\theta$$

On the interval $[0, \pi]$, the expansion $\cos(z \cos \theta) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k)!} z^{2k} \cos^{2k} \theta$ converges uniformly. For $a = \operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1/2$, we observe that:

$$\left| \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k)!} z^{2k} \cos^{2k} \theta \sin^{2\nu} \theta \right| \le \frac{|z|^{2k}}{(2k)!} \sin^{2a} \theta$$

Since $\int_0^\pi \sin^{2a} \theta \, d\theta < \infty$ and $\sum_{k \ge 0} \frac{|z|^{2k}}{(2k)!} < \infty$, the dominated convergence theorem justifies termwise integration:

$$\int_0^\pi \cos(z \cos \theta) \sin^{2\nu} \theta \, d\theta = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k)!} z^{2k} \int_0^\pi \cos^{2k} \theta \sin^{2\nu} \theta \, d\theta$$

Using symmetry and the beta integral (valid since $\operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1/2$):

$$\int_0^\pi \cos^{2k} \theta \sin^{2\nu} \theta \, d\theta = \frac{\Gamma(\nu+1/2)\Gamma(k+1/2)}{\Gamma(k+\nu+1)}$$

Substituting this into $F(\nu)$, we cancel the $\Gamma(\nu+1/2)$ term. Applying the identity $\Gamma(k+1/2) = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}(2k)!}{4^k k!}$, we obtain:

$$F(\nu) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{k! \Gamma(k+\nu+1)} \left(\frac{z}{2}\right)^{2k+\nu} = J_\nu(z)$$

This identity holds for $\operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1/2$. Because the right-hand side is an entire function of $\nu$, it constitutes the unique analytic continuation of $F(\nu)$. Thus, the continuation exists and equals $J_\nu(z)$ on $\operatorname{Re}(\nu) > -1$ (and indeed for all $\nu \in \mathbb{C}$).
