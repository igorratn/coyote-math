## Prompt

28. Two players, A and B, place a chess piece at each of two points on a straight line, with a distance of $L (L$ being a positive integer $)$ between them. A and B take turns to move their chess pieces along the line in any chosen direction, with movement distances of $a, b (a < b, a, b$ being positive integers), respectively. When the two chess pieces meet, A loses. Determine the necessary and sufficient conditions for B to have a winning strategy.

## Response 1

The two men are at the points $P_{A}(t),P_{B}(t)\in {\mathbf R}$,
$P_{B}(0)-P_{A}(0)=L>0$.  Put $d(t)=|P_{B}(t)-P_{A}(t)|\ge 0$.
If $P_{A}=P_{B}$ the play is over and, by the rule of the game,
$A$ is the loser.  Suppose that $A$ has the move and that the
present distance is $d>0$.  $A$ may replace $d$ by

$$
d_{A}=|d\pm a|=\begin{cases}
d+a & ({\rm the\ point\ is\ moved\ away\ from\ } B)\\
|d-a| & ({\rm the\ point\ is\ moved\ toward\ } B)
\end{cases}
\qquad (d_{A}=0\ {\rm is\ forbidden\ for\ }A),
$$

and $B$, who moves next, may replace a distance $d'>0$ by

$$
d_{B}=|d'\pm b|=\begin{cases}
d'+b\\
|d'-b|
\end{cases}
$$

and wins the moment he can make the choice $|d'-b|=0$.
If $d'\le b$ the second choice is possible, because the segment
that $B$ has to describe contains the point $P_{A}$; $B$ just walks
straight toward $A$ and the two men meet after the distance $d'$ has
been covered.  Hence

$$
d'>b\tag{1}
$$

is a necessary condition for the game to go on after $B$'s move.

Assume $b>a$ and that $B$ always walks straight toward $A$.
If at the beginning of a round the distance is $d$, $A$ can at best
increase it to $d+a$; condition (1) forces $d+a>b$, i.e. $d>b-a$.
After $B$'s answer the distance is

$$
d_{1}=d+a-b=d-(b-a)<d\tag{2}
$$

and (1) is again satisfied for the next round as long as $d_{1}>b-a$.
If $A$ on his turn walks toward $B$ the new distance is at most
$|d-a|$ and (2) is replaced by the even smaller number
$d_{1}'=|d-a|-b\le d-(a+b)$.  Consequently the choice

$$
d\longmapsto d+a\qquad{\rm (A\ walks\ away),\qquad }
d+a\longmapsto d+a-b\qquad{\rm (B\ walks\ toward\ A)}\tag{3}
$$

is the best that $A$ can do and it is the best that $B$ can do.
Put $d_{0}=L$ and let $d_{k}$ be the distance at the beginning of the
$k$-th move of $A$.  As long as the game lasts (3) gives, by induction,

$$
d_{k}=L-k\,(b-a),\qquad k=0,1,2,\dots\tag{4}
$$

The hypothesis $b>a$ makes the right–hand side a strictly decreasing
arithmetical progression of positive integers.  Hence there is a first
integer

$$
k_{0}=\Bigl\lceil\frac{L-b}{\,b-a\,}\Bigr\rceil+1\ge1\qquad
\Bigl(k_{0}\le\Bigl\lceil\frac{L}{b-a}\Bigr\rceil\Bigr)
$$

for which $d_{k_{0}}\le b$.  At the $k_{0}$-th move of $A$ we have,
by (4),

$$
d_{k_{0}-1}>b\ge d_{k_{0}-1}-(b-a),
$$

so $A$ is still able to choose the legal move
$d_{k_{0}-1}\mapsto d_{k_{0}-1}+a$, and after this move

$$
d' = d_{k_{0}-1}+a\le b+(b-a)+a=2b .
$$

In particular $d'\le b$ or else $b<d'\le b+(b-a)$.  In the first case
$B$ captures $A$ at once; in the second case $B$ walks toward $A$ and
after his $k_{0}$-th move the distance is

$$
d_{k_{0}} = d'-b = d_{k_{0}-1}-(b-a)\le b ,
$$

so that at the beginning of $A$'s $(k_{0}+1)$-st move the distance is
already $\le b$.  $A$ is then forced, on pain of walking into $B$,
to move away, putting the distance at $A$'s $(k_{0}+1)$-st move equal to
$d_{k_{0}}+a\le b+a$, and $B$'s next move, made straight toward $A$,
covers the remaining distance and the two men meet.  $A$ therefore
loses after at most $k_{0}+1$ complete rounds; with the choice (3) $B$
wins in the finite number of moves given by (4).

Hence, as soon as the step of $B$ is the larger, $B$ possesses a
forced win: keep walking straight toward $A$'s actual position.
The distance after each of $A$'s moves is $L+a$, $L+a-(b-a)$,
$L+a-2(b-a),\dots$; it becomes $\le b$ after finitely many steps and
the next move of $B$ is a meeting.  No choice of $A$ can do better,
because any move toward $B$ only diminishes the distance still more
rapidly.

If $b\le a$ the situation is reversed.  When $b=a$ the distance
$d_{k}$ in (4) stays constant, $d_{k}\equiv L$, and $A$, by always
moving away from $B$ in the same direction in which $B$ has just
moved, keeps the two points distinct for ever; $B$ has no winning play.
If $b<a$ the same strategy makes the distance increase by $a-b>0$
at every round, so $A$ not only avoids being caught but drives $B$
arbitrarily far away.  In particular, under the hypothesis of the
problem, $a<b$, the inequality $b>a$ is automatically fulfilled, and
consequently $B$ wins from every admissible initial position
$L\in{\mathbf N}$; the win is obtained after at most
$\lceil L/(b-a)\rceil$ pairs of moves.

In the customary discrete version of the game, in which a meeting is
counted only when a player lands exactly on the opponent's point,
the parity obstruction $y_{n+1}\equiv y_{n}\pm a\pmod{\gcd(a,b)}$
shows that $0$ can be reached only when $\gcd(a,b)\mid L$.  Write
$a=g\alpha$, $b=g\beta$ with $(\alpha,\beta)=1$.  A straightforward
retrograde analysis gives

$$
y\in{\cal W}_{B}\iff \exists\;\varepsilon=\pm1:
\;y+\varepsilon b=0\ {\rm or}\ y+\varepsilon b\in{\cal W}_{A},
\qquad
y\in{\cal W}_{A}\iff \forall\;\delta=\pm1,\;y+\delta a\ne0\Rightarrow
y+\delta a\in{\cal W}_{B},
$$

and the greatest fixed point of these two recursions is non-empty only
for $\beta=2\alpha$.  Hence a finite forced capture is possible only
when the two steps are in the exact ratio $2:1$.  After dividing by the
common divisor $g$ we may suppose $(a,b)=1$; then $B$ has a winning
strategy precisely for

$$
\,b=2a\qquad\text{and}\qquad a\mid L\,\qquad(L=ka,\;k\ge1),
$$

and the winning strategy is the same ''always walk toward $A$''; the
capture occurs after exactly $k$ moves of $B$.  If $b\ne2a$ (or, more
generally, if $\gcd(a,b)\nmid L$) $A$ can keep the distance forever
inside the safe residue class
$\{\,r\mid 0<r<b,\ r\not\equiv0\pmod a\,\}$ and the game goes on
indefinitely, so $B$ has no forced win.  In the usual continuous
interpretation the divisibility restriction disappears and the sole
necessary and sufficient condition for $B$ to force a meeting is the
strict inequality $b>a$, which is the hypothesis $a<b$ of the problem.
In particular, with the data of the statement $B$ always possesses a
winning strategy, and he may win by the simple policy described above.

## Response 2

The positions of the two men may be taken on the integer line.
Put the origin at (B)'s first position and let the (signed) distance
(x_{A}-x_{B}) at the beginning of a move of (A) be (s;(s\neq 0)).
When (A) moves he changes the distance to

$$
s_{1}=s\pm a ,\qquad
\begin{cases}
s_{1}=0 &\hbox{(A moves on B) } \\
|s_{1}|=b &\hbox{(B catches A at his next move)}
\end{cases}
\tag{1}
$$

are the two ways in which (A) loses at once.

If (|s_{1}|\neq b) and (s_{1}\neq0) the turn passes to (B); he changes the
distance to

$$
s' = s_{1}\mp b =s\pm a\mp b\in\Big{,s\pm (b-a),;s\pm (b+a)\Big}\tag{2}
$$

and the game goes on with the new starting distance (s').

Hence after every complete round the residue of the distance modulo
(b) is changed only by (A):

$$
s'\equiv s\pm a\pmod b .\tag{3}
$$

Put (d=\gcd (a,b),;a=d,a_{1},;b=d,b_{1},;(a_{1},b_{1})=1) and write
(s=d,\sigma ,\ L=d,\lambda;(\lambda\in\mathbb Z)).
Dividing (3) by (d),

$$
\sigma'\equiv\sigma\pm a_{1}\pmod{b_{1}} .\tag{4}
$$

Thus (A) moves the point (\sigma) on the cyclic graph
(\mathbf Z_{b_{1}}) by the steps (\pm a_{1}).
(B) wins at his move iff after (A)'s move the residue is
(0\pmod{b_{1}}) **and** the absolute value of the distance after that
move is exactly (b;(=d,b_{1})).

Consequently (A) can avoid being caught forever precisely when he can keep
the residue (\sigma;(\hbox{mod }b_{1})) away from (0) forever.
On the graph (\mathbf Z_{b_{1}}) the edges join
(i) to (i\pm a_{1};( \bmod b_{1})); because ((a_{1},b_{1})=1) the
graph is the ordinary (b_{1})-cycle.
Removing the vertex (0) leaves the path
(a_{1},2a_{1},\ldots ,(b_{1}-1)a_{1}) (indices mod (b_{1})).
On this path (A) can walk indefinitely, changing direction at the two
end–vertices, **unless the path has no edge**.

The path has no edge only when (b_{1}=2); then (a_{1}=1).

Hence

- if ((a_{1},b_{1})\neq(1,2)) (i.e. (b\neq2a)) the vertices distinct
from (0) form a path with at least one edge.

Starting from the residue (\lambda;(\bmod b_{1})) (if (\lambda\equiv0)
the first move of (A) makes it non-zero, because (a_{1}\neq0)) (A)
can forever move on that path, never making the residue (0) after his
move; therefore (|s_{1}|) is never (b) and (B) never catches (A).

- if (b_{1}=2) then (a_{1}=1); hence (b=2d) and (a=d), i.e.

$$
b=2a .\tag{5}
$$

The graph is (0!-!1!-!0); after deleting (0) one isolated vertex
remains.  Consequently (A) cannot make a move without putting the
residue equal to (0\pmod2).  Let the initial (signed) distance be
(s) with (d\mid s); otherwise (2) shows that (s) stays prime to
(d) and can never become (0), so (B) can never catch (A).
Assume then that

$$
a\mid L\qquad\bigl(L=d\lambda\bigr). \tag{6}
$$

If the beginning distance is (s=da) (mod (2a)) the two moves
(s\pm a) are (0) (A meets B) and (2a=b) (B catches A); (A) is lost.
If the beginning distance is (s=2ka;(k\ge1)) the move
(s-a=(2k-1)a) is safe; (B) answers with the move
(-b) and puts the distance at ((2k-1)a).  At the next round
(A) is at a distance ((2k-1)a); his two possible moves are
(2ka) (which returns the position) and ((2k-2)a).  By always moving
towards (A), (B) reduces (|k|) by one every two rounds, and after
finitely many rounds the distance becomes (a); (A) is then forced to lose.
Thus under (5)–(6) (B) catches (A) after at most (\lceil L/a\rceil)
moves: his winning strategy is always to step towards (A).

Consequently (B) possesses a (finite) forced win **iff** the two
numbers are in the ratio (1:2) and the initial separation is a multiple
of the slower step.

In the original notation ((d=\gcd(a,b))):

(B) wins (\iff b=2\gcd(a,b)) and (\gcd(a,b)\mid L); because
(a<b) this is simply

(b=2a) and (a\mid L).

If (b\neq2a) or (a\nmid L), (A) makes the distance after each of
his moves congruent to (a\pmod b) (congruent to (\pm a_{1}\pmod{b_{1}}))
and shuttles between the two neighbours of the residue (0) on the
(b_{1})-cycle; the distance never becomes (b) and (A) never steps on
(B).  The game goes on forever, so (B) has no winning strategy. ∎
