## Prompt
Consider the PDE

\[
\frac{\partial^\beta u}{\partial t^\beta} + a u u_x + b \frac{\partial^\beta}{\partial t^\beta} (u_{xxx}) = 0
\]
where \(a, b\) are nonzero constants and \(t > 0, 0 < β \leq 1\).

The traveling wave transformation is
\[
u(x, t) = U(\epsilon), \quad \epsilon = kx - \frac{1}{\beta} \left( ct + \frac{1}{\Gamma(\beta)} \right)^\beta
\]

By Ritz-like method, we seek a solitary wave solution in this form
\[
U(\epsilon) = A \operatorname{sech^2}(B\epsilon)
\]
Determine the ratio \(\frac{A}{B}\) for \(b<0\) and \(B>0\).

## Response 1
The equation

$$
U^{(\beta)}_{t}+a\,U\,U_{x}+b\,\big(U_{xxx}\big)^{(\beta)}_{t}=0 ,\qquad 
0<\beta\le 1,
\qquad 
U=U(\varepsilon ),\qquad 
\varepsilon =k\,x-\frac1{\beta}\Big(ct+\frac1{\Gamma (\beta)}\Big)^{\!\beta},
$$

$k\neq 0$, $c\neq 0$, is reduced by the fractional chain rule  

$$
\frac{\partial^{\beta}}{\partial t^{\beta}}F(\varepsilon)=
F'(\varepsilon )\,\frac{\partial^{\beta}\varepsilon}{\partial t^{\beta}}
= -\,c\,F'(\varepsilon ),\qquad 
\frac{\partial}{\partial x}F(\varepsilon)=k\,F'(\varepsilon ),
$$

to the ordinary differential equation (the prime denotes $d/d\varepsilon$)

\begin{equation}
 -c\,U' +a\,k\,U\,U' -b\,c\,k^{3}\,U'''=0,
\qquad '\equiv\frac{d}{d\varepsilon},
\tag{1}
\end{equation}
in which the mixed derivative has been written as
$\,\partial_{t}^{\beta}U_{xxx}= -c\,k^{3}U'''$.
Upon integration once, and imposing the solitary-wave
boundary conditions $U,U',U''\to0$ as $|\varepsilon|\to\infty$,
(1) becomes the second-order stationary equation

\begin{equation}
 -c\,U+\frac{a\,k}{2}\,U^{2}-b\,c\,k^{3}\,U''=0 .
\tag{2}
\end{equation}

Put the single–humped trial function

$$
U_{0}(\varepsilon)=A\,\operatorname{sech}^{2}\!\big(B\varepsilon\big)
\equiv A\,s^{2},\qquad 
s:=\operatorname{sech}(B\varepsilon),\; 
t:=\tanh(B\varepsilon),
\tag{3}
$$

with an (unknown) amplitude $A\neq0$ and an inverse width $B>0$.
For (3)

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
U_{0}'&=-2AB\,s^{2}t,\\
U_{0}''&=4B^{2}A\,s^{2}-6B^{2}A\,s^{4}
      =4B^{2}U_{0}-\frac{6B^{2}}{A}\,U_{0}^{2},\\
U_{0}'''&=\Bigl(4B^{2}-\frac{12B^{2}}{A}U_{0}\Bigr)U_{0}' ,
\end{aligned}
\tag{4}
\end{equation}
so that (2) is satisfied identically in $s$ if the coefficients of
the independent functions $s^{2}$ and $s^{4}$ vanish separately.
Using (4) in (2),

$$
\Bigl[-cA-4bc\,k^{3}B^{2}A\Bigr]s^{2}
+\Bigl[\frac{ak}{2}A^{2}+6bc\,k^{3}B^{2}A\Bigr]s^{4}\equiv0,
$$

hence

\begin{align}
 -\,c\Bigl[\,1+4\,b\,k^{3}B^{2}\Bigr]&=0, \tag{5a}\\
 \frac{a\,k}{2}A+6\,b\,c\,k^{3}B^{2}&=0. \tag{5b}
\end{align}

For a non-trivial solitary wave $c\neq0$, (5a) gives the
(nonlinear) dispersion relation

\begin{equation}
 4\,b\,k^{3}B^{2}+1=0,
\qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad 
 B^{2}= -\,\frac{1}{\,4\,b\,k^{3}\,}>0,
\tag{6}
\end{equation}
which is real precisely when the dispersive coefficient is
negative, $b<0$ (the ordinary Korteweg–de Vries case $b>0$ is
recovered by taking the (fractional) phase speed $c<0$).  In the
usual normalisation $k=1$ (i.e. $\varepsilon=x-
\beta^{-1}(ct+1/\Gamma(\beta))^{\beta}$) the inverse width of the
$\operatorname{sech}^{2}$ hump is

\begin{equation}
 B=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{-\,b}}\,,\qquad b<0,\qquad B>0,
\tag{7}
\end{equation}
indeed $B=1/2$ for the canonical KdV ($b=-1$).

Using (6) in (5b) the $B$–dependence drops out of the
nonlinear term and the solitary-wave amplitude is

\begin{equation}
 A=\frac{3c}{a\,k},
\tag{8}
\end{equation}
the familiar KdV result $A=3c/a$ (for $k=1$); the sign of $A$
is the sign of $c/a$.  In particular, for the forward-propagating
($c>0$) elevation soliton ($a>0$) the amplitude is positive,
$A=3c/(ak)>0$, while the width (6)–(7) is controlled entirely by
the (negative) dispersion $b$.

Eliminating the (inessential) phase speed $c$ between (6)–(8) the
amplitude can be written solely in terms of the width,

\begin{equation}
 A=\frac{12\,|b|\,k^{2}}{a}\,B^{2},
\qquad b=-|b|<0,
\tag{9}
\end{equation}
the usual KdV balance $A\propto B^{2}$; indeed, for $k=1$ and
$a=1$, $A=12|b|B^{2}$, i.e. $A=3$ and $B=1/2$ when $|b|=1$.

The ratio of the soliton amplitude to its inverse width follows
from (6)–(8):

\begin{equation}
\frac{A}{B}
= \frac{\,3c/(ak)\,}
       {\displaystyle\sqrt{-\frac{1}{4bk^{3}}}}
= \frac{3c}{a\,k}\,
  \sqrt{-\,4\,b\,k^{3}}
= \frac{6c}{a}\,\sqrt{-\,b\,k}\;,
\qquad b<0,\;B>0,
\tag{10}
\end{equation}
so that $|A|/B>0$ for the physical (elevation) soliton
($a\,c>0$).  The $k$–dependence is a trivial stretching of the
$\varepsilon$-coordinate; with the customary choice of unit wave
number, $k=1$ (i.e. $\varepsilon=x- \beta^{-1}(ct+1/\Gamma)^{\beta}$),
(10) simplifies to

\begin{equation}
\frac{A}{B}
= \frac{\,6\,c\,\sqrt{-\,b}\,}{a},
\qquad b<0,
\;
\tag{11}
\end{equation}
and, if the fractional phase speed is normalised to unity,
$c=1$, the amplitude–to–width ratio depends only on the
nonlinear-to-dispersive balance

\begin{equation}
%
\frac{A}{B}= \frac{6}{a}\,\sqrt{-b}\,\qquad(b<0,\;k=c=1), 
\end{equation}
the sign being that of $a$.  In the most common normalisation
$a=1$ (the standard KdV nonlinearity) this is simply

\begin{equation}
\frac{A}{B}=6\,\sqrt{-b}\;\; (>0),
\qquad b<0,\;k=c=a=1,
\tag{12}
\end{equation}
so that the $\operatorname{sech}^{2}$ solitary wave
satisfies the familiar “amplitude $\approx$ (width)$^{-2}$” rule.
For the canonical unit-coefficient KdV, $a=1,\;b=-1$, the
single-soliton is

$$
U_{0}(\varepsilon)=3c\,\operatorname{sech}^{2}
\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{c}}{2}\,\varepsilon\right),\qquad
\varepsilon=x-\frac1\beta\Bigl(ct+\frac1{\Gamma(\beta)}\Bigr)^{\!\beta},
$$

i.e. $A=3c$ and $B=\sqrt{c}/2$; with the conventional unit speed
$c=1$ the soliton is $U_{0}=3\operatorname{sech}^{2}(\varepsilon/2)$,
hence $A=3$, $B=1/2$ and the (positive) amplitude-to-inverse-width
ratio is the universal KdV number

\[
\frac{|A|}{B}=6,
\]

the negative sign ($A/B=-6$) simply reflecting the choice $b=-1<0$
in the convention $u_{t}+a uu_{x}+b u_{xxx}=0$.  If the minus sign
is absorbed in the definition of $b$ (i.e. $b>0$ is the dispersive
coefficient in $u_{t}+a uu_{x}-b u_{xxx}=0$), the ratio is
$|A|/B=6\sqrt{b}/a$, and for the textbook KdV $a=6,\;b=1$ the
familiar $A=2B^{2}$ ($|A|/B=2B$) is recovered.

In particular, discarding the inessential scale factors
($k=1$, $c=1$, $a=1$) the Ritz (variational) balance gives the
compact, often-quoted result

\[
A = 12|b|\,B^{2},\qquad 
\frac{|A|}{B}=12|b|\,B=6\sqrt{|b|},
\]

so that for the standard (unit-dispersion) solitary wave,
$|b|=1$, the amplitude is exactly six times the inverse width,
$|A|/B=6$ ($A$ and $B$ having opposite signs if the equation is
written with the dispersive term $+b\,u_{xxx}$, $b<0$).  The
negative sign in $A/B=-6$ for $b=-1$ is a mere consequence of the
sign convention; the physically relevant (positive) ratio of the
soliton height to its reciprocal width is $6$.  If the
normalisations $k\neq1$ or $c\neq1$ are retained, the invariant
combination is (10), i.e. $A/B\propto c\sqrt{-b\,k}/a$. ∎

## Response 2
By putting

\begin{equation}
u(x,t)=U(\epsilon),\qquad 
\epsilon = k\,x-\frac{1}{\beta}
\Bigl(ct+\frac{1}{\Gamma (\beta)}\Bigr)^{\!\beta}\qquad(0<\beta\le 1),
\tag{1}
\end{equation}

and using the well-known rule
(Atangana’s “$\beta$”–derivative, the conformable derivative, the
modified Riemann–Liouville derivative, etc.)

\begin{equation}
{}^{J\!}D_{t}^{\beta}\,U\bigl(\epsilon(x,t)\bigr)
=
\frac{dU}{d\epsilon}\;{}^{J\!}D_{t}^{\beta}\epsilon
= -\,c\,U'(\epsilon),\qquad '\equiv\frac{d}{d\epsilon},
\tag{2}
\end{equation}

the time-fractional equation

\begin{equation}
{}^{J\!}D_{t}^{\beta}u
+a\,u\,u_{x}+b\,{}^{J\!}D_{t}^{\beta}\!\left(u_{xxx}\right)=0,
\qquad a\,b\neq0,
\tag{3}
\end{equation}

becomes, with $u_{x}=kU'$ and $u_{xxx}=k^{3}U'''$,

\begin{equation}
-c\,U' + a k\,U\,U' - b\,c\,k^{3}\,U^{IV}=0,
\qquad U^{IV}\equiv\frac{d^{4}U}{d\epsilon^{4}} .
\tag{4}
\end{equation}

For a localized solitary wave $U(\pm\infty)=U'(\pm\infty)=
U''(\pm\infty)=U'''(\pm\infty)=0$ we integrate (4) once and get the
third–order stationary equation

\begin{equation}
-\,c\,U+\frac{a k}{2}\,U^{2}-b\,c\,k^{3}\,U''=0,
\qquad U''\equiv\frac{d^{2}U}{d\epsilon^{2}},
\tag{5}
\end{equation}
the integration constant being put equal to zero.
Eq. (5) is the Euler–Lagrange equation of the functional

\begin{equation}
J[U]=\int_{-\infty }^{+\infty}
\!L\bigl(U,U'\bigr)\,d\epsilon ,\qquad
L\bigl(U,U'\bigr)=
-\,\frac{\,b\,c\,k^{3}}{2}\,(U')^{2}
-\frac{c}{2}\,U^{2}
+\frac{a\,k}{6}\,U^{3} .
\tag{6}
\end{equation}

Indeed,
$\; \displaystyle
\delta J=0\Longleftrightarrow 
\frac{\partial L}{\partial U}
-\frac{d}{d\epsilon}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial U'}=0\,$
is precisely (5).

In the Ritz (Rayleigh–Ritz) approximation we take as a trial function
the one–hump symmetric function

\begin{equation}
U_{0}(\epsilon)=A\;{\rm sech}^{2}(B\epsilon),\qquad
A\neq0,\;B>0,
\tag{7}
\end{equation}

which satisfies the required boundary conditions.
Putting (7) in (6) and using

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\infty }^{\infty}{\rm sech}^{4}(B\epsilon)\,d\epsilon
&=\frac{4}{3B},\\[2pt]
\int_{-\infty }^{\infty}{\rm sech}^{6}(B\epsilon)\,d\epsilon
&=\frac{16}{15B},\\[2pt]
\int_{-\infty }^{\infty}
\Bigl[\frac{d}{d\epsilon}{\rm sech}^{2}(B\epsilon)\Bigr]^{2}\!d\epsilon
&=\frac{16}{15}\,A^{2}B ,
\end{aligned}
\tag{8}
\end{equation}

the averaged (or “Ritz”) action becomes an ordinary function of the
two variational parameters $A$ and $B$,

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
J(A,B)&=
\int_{-\infty }^{\infty}L\bigl(U_{0},U_{0}'\bigr)\,d\epsilon  \\
&= -\,\frac{8\,b\,c\,k^{3}}{15}\,A^{2}B
-\frac{2\,c}{3}\,\frac{A^{2}}{B}
+\frac{8\,a\,k}{45}\,\frac{A^{3}}{B} .
\end{aligned}
\tag{9}
\end{equation}

The Ritz (stationarity) conditions
$\;\partial J/\partial A=0,\;\partial J/\partial B=0\;$ give the two
algebraic equations

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
&\frac{\partial J}{\partial A}\equiv
-\frac{16\,b\,c\,k^{3}}{15}\,A\,B
-\frac{4\,c}{3}\,\frac{A}{B}
+\frac{8\,a\,k}{15}\,\frac{A^{2}}{B}=0,\\[4pt]
&\frac{\partial J}{\partial B}\equiv
-\frac{8\,b\,c\,k^{3}}{15}\,A^{2}
+\frac{2\,c}{3}\,\frac{A^{2}}{B^{2}}
-\frac{8\,a\,k}{45}\,\frac{A^{3}}{B^{2}}=0 .
\end{aligned}
\right.
\tag{10}
\end{equation}

After cancelling the common non–zero factor $A$ the system (10) reduces
to

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
8\,a\,k\,A &=16\,b\,c\,k^{3}B^{2}+20\,c,\\
8\,a\,k\,A &=-24\,b\,c\,k^{3}B^{2}+30\,c,
\end{aligned}
\tag{11}
\end{equation}
whose simultaneous solution is

\begin{equation}
\,4\,b\,k^{3}B^{2}=1\,,\qquad
\,A=\frac{3\,c}{a\,k}\,,
\tag{12}
\end{equation}
the trivial solution $A\equiv0$ being discarded.
Equation (12)$_{1}$ is the “nonlinear dispersion relation’’ that the
sech$^{2}$ trial function imposes on the width $B$; it is independent of
the wave speed $c$, a direct consequence of the fact that the
fractional derivative acts also on the dispersive term $u_{xxx}$.
In particular, with the normalization $k=1$,

\begin{equation}
B^{2}=\frac{1}{4\,b},\qquad
A=\frac{3c}{a},
\tag{13}
\end{equation}
and the solitary wave (1)–(7) is

\begin{equation}
u(x,t)\;\approx\;
\frac{3c}{a\,k}\;
\operatorname{sech}^{2}\!
\Biggl[
\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\,b\,k^{3}}}\,
\Biggl(
 k\,x-\frac{1}{\beta}\Bigl(ct+\frac{1}{\Gamma(\beta)}\Bigr)^{\!\beta}
\Biggr)
\Biggr],
\tag{14}
\end{equation}
which indeed satisfies (5) in the Ritz (integral) sense,
$\;\delta J=0$.

If the (complex) root is chosen so that the physical width $B$ is real
and positive, $b$ must be taken with the sign that makes $b\,k^{3}>0$ in
(12)$_{1}$.  In the usual “focusing’’ case the dispersive constant is
negative, $b=-|b|<0$, and it is convenient to write (12)$_{1}$ as

\begin{equation}
B=\frac{1}{2\,k^{3/2}\sqrt{-b}}\;>0,
\qquad (b<0),
\tag{15}
\end{equation}
the sign of the square root being chosen so that $B>0$.
The (signed) amplitude-to-inverse-width ratio follows directly from
(12):

\begin{equation}
\frac{A}{B}
=
\frac{\,3c/(a k)\,}{\,1\big/\!\big(2\sqrt{b\,k^{3}}\big)}
=
\frac{6\,c}{a}\,\sqrt{\,b\,k\,}
=
\frac{6\,c}{a\,k}\;B^{-1},
\tag{16}
\end{equation}
and, with the real (principal) root for $b<0$ ($-b=|b|>0$),

\begin{equation}
\,
\frac{A}{B}
=
\frac{6\,c}{a}\,\sqrt{-\,b\,k}\;\;,
\qquad 
\Bigl|\frac{A}{B}\Bigr|
=
\frac{6\,|c|}{|a|}\,\sqrt{|b|\,k}\;,
\qquad b<0,\;B>0,
\,
\tag{17}
\end{equation}
the overall sign being the sign of $A$, i.e. the sign of $c/a$.
In the most frequently used normalizations $k=1$ (unit wave-number)
and $|c|=1$ (unit wave-speed) the magnitude of the ratio depends only
on the material constants,

\begin{equation}
\,\Bigl|\frac{A}{B}\Bigr|
=
\frac{6}{|a|}\,\sqrt{|b|}
=
\frac{6}{|a|}\,\sqrt{-\,b}\qquad(b<0,\;k=|c|=1),\,
\tag{18}
\end{equation}
and the solitary wave has the familiar $sech^{2}$ shape with
$A$ proportional to the wave speed and $B$ independent of it.
The negative sign in (17)–(18) reflects the fact that for $b<0$ a
bright ($A>0$) solitary wave requires $a$ and $c$ to have opposite
signs; if the polarity is chosen so that $A>0$ the (positive) ratio is
simply $|A|/B = 6|c|\sqrt{-b k}/|a|$.

In particular, if the extra factor $c$ that comes from the
fractional time-derivative of the dispersive term $u_{xxx}$ is formally
dropped (the conventional reduction $^{J\!}D_{t}^{\beta}u_{xxx}\to
k^{3}U'''$), Eq. (5) becomes the ordinary stationary KdV equation

\begin{equation}
-\,c\,U+\frac{a k}{2}U^{2}+b k^{3}U''=0,
\tag{19}
\end{equation}
and the Ritz procedure gives the classic KdV $sech^{2}$ soliton

\begin{equation}
A=\frac{3c}{a k},\qquad
B=\sqrt{-\frac{c}{4\,b\,k^{3}}}\;(>0),\qquad b<0,
\tag{20}
\end{equation}
so that the amplitude-to-width ratio is

\begin{equation}
\,
\frac{A}{B}= \frac{3c}{a k}\,
\Bigg/\sqrt{-\frac{c}{4bk^{3}}}
=
\frac{6}{a}\,\sqrt{-b\,c\,k}\;,
\qquad
\Bigl|\frac{A}{B}\Bigr|
=
\frac{6}{|a|}\sqrt{|b|\,|c|\,k}\,,
\qquad b<0,
\,
\tag{21}
\end{equation}
the sign being that of $A$.  With the usual normalizations $k=1$ and
$|c|=1$ this reduces to the textbook result

\begin{equation}
|A|=\frac{3|c|}{|a|},\qquad 
B=\frac12\sqrt{\frac{|c|}{|b|}},\qquad
\frac{|A|}{B}= \frac{6}{|a|}\sqrt{|b|\,|c|},
\tag{22}
\end{equation}
and for a right-moving wave ($c>0$) with $a>0$ and the customary
focusing dispersion $b=-|b|<0$,

\[
\,\displaystyle
\frac{A}{B}= \frac{6}{a}\sqrt{-b\,c}\;(>0)\,
\qquad\Bigl(\;|A|/B=6\sqrt{|b|c}/a\;\Bigr),
\]

so that in the completely normalized case $c=k=1$

\[
\,|A|=\frac{3}{a},\qquad B=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{|b|}},\qquad
\frac{|A|}{B}= \frac{6\sqrt{|b|}}{a}= \frac{6\sqrt{-b}}{a}\,,
\]

the minus sign under the root reminding that the physical (real)
width requires $b<0$.  The signed ratio is $A/B=-6\sqrt{-b}/a$ if the
conventional KdV polarity ($A>0$ for $a>0$, $b<0$) is kept; the magnitude
(18) is the quantity usually quoted.  Retaining the wave-number $k$,
the compact form is $|A|/B = (6/|a|)\sqrt{|b|\,k}$ (times $|c|^{1/2}$ if
the speed is not scaled to unity).  The $sech^{2}$ trial function
therefore produces a genuine solitary wave of amplitude proportional to
the wave speed and of width proportional to $1/\sqrt{|b|}$, the
characteristic KdV scaling, now modulated by the fractional travelling
coordinate (1).  If the full fractional factor $-b c k^{3}$ is kept in
the reduced ODE (5), the width (15) becomes independent of the wave
speed and the ratio is given by (17), i.e. $|A|/B = 6|c|\sqrt{|b|k}/|a|$,
which for $k=1$ is the particularly simple $ |A|/B = 6|c|\sqrt{|b|}/|a|
= 6|c|\sqrt{-b}/|a|$ ($b<0$).  The $c$-independent (text-book) form
(21)–(22) is recovered by the customary reduction $b\,c\,k^{3}\to b\,k^{3}$. ∎
