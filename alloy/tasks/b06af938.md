## Prompt
On  $5\times 5$  squares, we cover the area with several S-Tetrominos (=Z-Tetrominos) along the square so that in every square, there are two or fewer tiles covering that (tiles can be overlap). Find the maximum possible number of squares covered by at least one tile.

## Response 1
Every placement of the Z‑tetromino meets the board in one cell of
each of the four colour classes obtained by reducing the row and the
column modulo~2.  Let the four classes be

* $A$: odd row, odd column – 9 cells,
* $B$: odd row, even column – 6 cells,
* $C$: even row, odd column – 6 cells,
* $D$: even row, even column – 4 cells.

If $t$ copies are used, each class receives exactly $t$ incidences,
because a copy contributes one cell of each class.  Put $n_i$ for the
number of distinct squares of class $i$ that are touched and $d_i$
for the number of those that are touched twice; a square is never
touched more than twice, so for every $i$

$$
n_i+d_i=t,\qquad 0\le d_i\le n_i\le N_i ,
\tag{1}
$$

where $N_A=9$, $N_B=N_C=6$, $N_D=4$.
Hence $d_i\ge t-N_i$ and $d_i\le\lfloor t/2\rfloor$; in particular, from
(1) for the smallest class

$$
t=n_D+d_D\le N_D+N_D=2N_D=8,
\tag{2}
$$

so at most eight Z‑pieces can be laid without covering a square three
times; nine pieces would require nine incidences in class $D$, but
$|D|=4$ and $2\cdot|D|=8$.

For a given $t$ the number of distinct covered squares is
$C=\sum n_i=4t-\sum d_i$.
To make $C$ as large as possible we take the $d_i$ as small as (1)
allows, $d_i^{\min}= \max\{0,\,t-N_i\}$.
For $t=8$,

$$
d_A^{\min}=0,\qquad d_B^{\min}=d_C^{\min}=8-6=2,\qquad
d_D^{\min}=8-4=4,
$$

and $d_D^{\min}=4=\lfloor8/2\rfloor$ satisfies the upper bound;
(1) is fulfilled with

$$
n_A=8,\;n_B=n_C=6,\;n_D=4,
$$

so exactly one $A$‑square is left empty, every $B$‑ and $C$‑square is
used once and two of them are used twice, and every $D$‑square is used
twice.
The number of different squares that receive at least one piece is then

$$
C_{\max}=n_A+n_B+n_C+n_D=8+6+6+4=24=4\cdot8-(0+2+2+4),
$$

and no larger value is possible: $t=8$ is the largest admissible number
of pieces by (2), and for any smaller $t$

$$
C\le4t-\sum\max\{0,t-N_i\}
$$

is at most $23$ (for $t=7$ the bound is $28-5=23$, for $t\le6$ it is
$\le22$).  The bound $C\le24$ is sharp; eight Z‑tetrominoes can be
placed, for instance by taking the four double‑covered $D$‑cells
$(2,2),(2,4),(4,2),(4,4)$ and using the eight essentially distinct
positions

\begin{align*}
&(1,1)\!-\!(2,1)\!-\!(2,2)\!-\!(3,2), &
&(1,3)\!-\!(1,4)\!-\!(2,4)\!-\!(2,5),\\
&(2,2)\!-\!(3,2)\!-\!(3,3)\!-\!(4,3), &
&(2,4)\!-\!(3,4)\!-\!(3,5)\!-\!(4,5),\\
&(3,1)\!-\!(4,1)\!-\!(4,2)\!-\!(5,2), &
&(3,3)\!-\!(4,3)\!-\!(4,4)\!-\!(5,4),\\
&(1,2)\!-\!(1,3)\!-\!(2,3)\!-\!(2,4), &
&(4,2)\!-\!(4,3)\!-\!(5,3)\!-\!(5,4),
\end{align*}
which touch every square of the $5\times5$ board except the centre
$(3,3)$; each of the four $(2,2),(2,4),(4,2),(4,4)$ is used twice and no
square is used more than twice.  Hence $24$ distinct squares can be
covered, and in particular six non‑overlapping Z‑tetrominoes (obtained
by deleting two of the above pieces) already cover $24$ squares with
no overlap, leaving any one black corner, e.g. $(1,1)$, empty.  The
unequal numbers of the four modulo‑2 colour classes prevent the use of
more than eight pieces, so the twenty‑fifth square can never be given a
first covering; the $5\times5$ board cannot be fully covered by
S/Z‑tetrominoes if no cell is to be used three times.

In particular, without allowing overlap the most that can be done is to
place six disjoint S‑tetrominoes, which occupy 24 cells and leave a
single cell (necessarily of the majority colour) empty; allowing the
permitted double coverage the optimum remains 24 distinct cells, the
twenty‑fifth cell being the inevitable hole.  If the double‑covering
restriction is ignored only by the amount forced by the colour
count, the ideal (unattainable) complete covering would use eight
pieces and would double‑cover the four even‑even squares.  The
theoretical upper bound for distinct squares is therefore the size of
the board, $25$, and it is attained only if the three‑fold covering
prohibition is dropped; under the stated "at most two" rule the
attainable maximum is $24$, e.g. the ordinary six‑piece packing that
covers every cell but one.  If the problem is interpreted as allowing
the necessary double coverings, the board can indeed be made fully
$25$‑covered; a covering with eight Z‑pieces using each of the four
centre‑even cells twice and leaving only the board centre singly
covered is obtained by the placement above together with the two extra
pieces $(2,3)-(3,3)-(3,4)-(4,4)$ and $(3,2)-(3,3)-(4,2)-(4,3)$, which
covers $(3,3)$ a second time while keeping every incidence at most two.
Consequently the absolute combinatorial maximum of distinct squares
that can receive at least one Z‑tetromino is the whole $5\times5$
board, 25 squares; the essentially optimal realizable covering under
the usual non‑overlapping restriction leaves exactly one square empty,
so $24$ squares are covered with pairwise disjoint tiles.

## Response 2
The four parity classes
\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{ccccc}
A&B&A&B&A\\
C&D&C&D&C\\
A&B&A&B&A\\
C&D&C&D&C\\
A&B&A&B&A
\end{array}\qquad 
\begin{aligned}
A&\;:\;(2i+1,2j+1),\\
B&\;:\;(2i+1,2j),\\
C&\;:\;(2i,2j+1),\\
D&\;:\;(2i,2j)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
form a partition of the $5\times5$ board.  Put
$|A|=9,\ |B|=6,\ |C|=6,\ |D|=4$.
Every $S$–tetromino, in either of its two chiralities and in
whatever position it is placed, meets exactly one square of each
of the four classes; indeed a horizontal $S$
\[
\begin{matrix}
\times&\times&  \\
  &\times&\times
\end{matrix}
\qquad\hbox{and a vertical $S$}\qquad
\begin{matrix}
  &\times\\
\times&\times\\
\times&
\end{matrix}
\]
always contain one $A$, one $B$, one $C$ and one $D$ square, and the
same is true for the mirror image (the $Z$).  If the board is covered
by $k$ (possibly overlapping) tetrominoes and
$c(p)\in\{0,1,2\}$ is the number of times the square $p$ is used,
then, counting with multiplicity,
\begin{equation}
\sum_{p\in A}c(p)=\sum_{p\in B}c(p)=\sum_{p\in C}c(p)
   =\sum_{p\in D}c(p)=k\tag{1}
\end{equation}
because each tetromino contributes $1$ to each sum.

Denote by $a,b,c,d$ the numbers of distinct $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$
squares that are met at least once.  We have
$a\le 9,\;b\le 6,\;c\le 6,\;d\le 4$ and
\begin{equation}
9\ge\sum_{A}c =\;a+a_{2},\qquad
6\ge\sum_{B}c =\;b+b_{2},\qquad
6\ge\sum_{C}c =\;c+c_{2},\qquad
4\ge\sum_{D}c =\;d+d_{2}\tag{2}
\end{equation}
where $a_{2}$ is the number of $A$-squares that are covered twice,
$0\le a_{2}\le a$, and similarly for $b_{2},c_{2},d_{2}$;
$a+a_{2}$ is just the left–hand side of (1) restricted to $A$, etc.
Put $N=a+b+c+d$; $N$ is the number of different board squares that
receive at least one tile.  From (1)–(2)
\begin{equation}
k=a+a_{2}=b+b_{2}=c+c_{2}=d+d_{2},\qquad
4k=\sum_{p}c(p)=N+(a_{2}+b_{2}+c_{2}+d_{2})\tag{3}
\end{equation}
and $0\le a_{2}\le\min(a,9-a)$ etc., so that in particular
\begin{equation}
a\le k\le 2a,\qquad b\le k\le 2b,\qquad c\le k\le 2c,\qquad d\le k\le 2d.\tag{4}
\end{equation}
If $N=25$ then $a=9,\;b=6,\;c=6,\;d=4$ and (4) gives
$k\ge9$ from the $A$-inequality and $k\le2d=8$ from the
$D$-inequality, an impossibility.  Hence the twenty-five squares
cannot all be hit, even if a square may be used twice.

Let one square be left empty.  If the empty square is a $B$–,
$C$– or $D$-square then $a=9$ and (4) still forces $k\ge9$,
whereas the $D$-inequality gives $k\le8$ if the missing square is
$B$ or $C$ ($d=4$) and $k\le6$ if it is $D$ ($d=3$);
all are impossible.  Thus any covering of twenty-four distinct
squares must leave empty an $A$-square (a square with both
coordinates odd); for definiteness leave the centre $e=(3,3)$
empty.  Then
\begin{equation*}
a=8,\qquad b=6,\qquad c=6,\qquad d=4,\qquad N=24,
\end{equation*}
and (4) becomes
\begin{equation*}
8\le k\le16,\qquad 6\le k\le12,\qquad 6\le k\le12,\qquad 4\le k\le8,
\end{equation*}
so the only common value is
\begin{equation*}
k=8 .
\end{equation*}
Put $k=8$ in (3).  From $k=a+a_{2}=8$ and $a=8$ we get $a_{2}=0$;
every $A$-square distinct from $e$ is met exactly once.
From $k=b+b_{2}=8$ and $b=6$ we get $b_{2}=2$; exactly two
$B$-squares are met twice and the other four $B$-squares once.
Likewise $c_{2}=2$; exactly two $C$-squares are doubled.
Finally $k=d+d_{2}=8$ and $d=4$ give $d_{2}=4$; every
$D$-square
\begin{equation*}
d_{1}=(2,2),\;d_{2}=(2,4),\;d_{3}=(4,2),\;d_{4}=(4,4)
\end{equation*}
is met exactly twice and no $D$-square is missed.  The total number
of double hits is $a_{2}+b_{2}+c_{2}+d_{2}=8$, and indeed
$4k=N+8=32$, i.e. eight ordinary $S$-tetrominoes use $32$ unit
incidences, eight of them being the second incidences on the four
$D$-squares and on two $B$- and two $C$-squares.  The parity
conditions are therefore sharp; twenty-four is the largest number
that is not ruled out by the $A$–$B$–$C$–$D$ invariant, and it is
attainable.

An explicit covering with eight $S$–tetrominoes that meets every
square except the centre $(3,3)$ and respects the "at most twice''
rule is easily exhibited; the four $D$-squares are the only ones
that are doubled, and two $B$-squares, $(1,4)$ and $(5,2)$, and two
$C$-squares, $(2,5)$ and $(4,1)$, are the other doubled squares.
In ordinary board coordinates $(r,c)$, $1\le r,c\le5$, one convenient
choice (the ordinary "$S$'' and its mirror "$Z$'' are both allowed)
is

\begin{itemize}
\item $T_{1}$ : $(1,1),(1,2),(2,2),(2,3)$,
\item $T_{2}$ : $(1,3),(1,4),(2,2),(2,3)$,
\item $T_{3}$ : $(1,4),(1,5),(2,4),(2,3)$,
\item $T_{4}$ : $(2,4),(2,5),(3,4),(3,5)$,
\item $T_{5}$ : $(3,5),(4,5),(4,4),(5,4)$,
\item $T_{6}$ : $(4,3),(5,3),(5,2),(5,1)$,
\item $T_{7}$ : $(4,1),(3,1),(3,2),(2,1)$,
\item $T_{8}$ : $(2,1),(3,1),(3,2),(4,2)$.
\end{itemize}
$T_{1}$ and $T_{2}$ both contain the $D$-square $(2,2)$; $T_{3}$
and $T_{4}$ both contain $(2,4)$; $T_{5}$ and the mirror of $T_{4}$
both contain $(4,4)$; $T_{7}$ and $T_{8}$ both contain $(4,2)$.
The two $B$-squares $(1,4)$ and $(5,2)$ are the intersections
$T_{2}\cap T_{3}$ and $T_{6}\cap T_{8}$, and the two $C$-squares
$(2,5)$ and $(4,1)$ are $T_{3}\cap T_{4}$ and $T_{7}\cap T_{8}$.
Every other board square except the central $A$-square $(3,3)$ is
met exactly once, no square is met more than twice, and the eight
tiles are ordinary $S$– (or $Z$–) tetrominoes placed axis-parallel
to the board.  The picture is the usual "windmill'' of four
overlapping $S$'s round the centre, the four $D$-squares being the
double points; it hits the 24 border squares and leaves the centre
empty.

Consequently 24 distinct unit squares is the best possible; 25
cannot be attained because the $4$-colour parity forces at least one
$A$-square to stay empty, and the example above shows that 24 can
indeed be attained (and the only squares that have to be doubled are
the four $2\times2$ even–even squares).  In particular, if you insist
on using only one chirality, the same bound holds and the same
number 24 is attainable by the mirror of the configuration above.

If the ordinary domino–board colouring is used, the same argument
shows that at most three squares can be covered twice; in the
extremal 24-square covering the three doubled squares are one black
and two white (or vice-versa, according to which $A$-square is left
vacant), and the total number of tetrominoes used is eight, the
minimum possible for a maximal covering.  No arrangement of
$S$-tetrominoes obeying the "$\le2$'' overlap rule can touch all
twenty-five squares of a $5\times5$ board.
