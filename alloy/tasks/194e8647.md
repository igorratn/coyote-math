## Prompt
For a positive integer $x>1$, define set $S_{x}=\{p^{\alpha} \mid p$ is a prime factor of $x, 0 \leq \alpha \leq v_{p}(x), \alpha \equiv v_{p}(x) \pmod{2}\}$. Here $v_{p}(x)$ denotes the power of $p$ in the standard factorization of $x$. Also let $f(x)$ be the sum of all the elements in $S_{x}$, and define $f(1)=1$.

For given positive integer $m$. Suppose positive integer sequence $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n, \ldots$ satisfies that for any integer $n > m$,
$$a_{n+1} = \max\{f(a_n), f(a_{n-1}+1), \ldots, f(a_{n-m}+m)\}.$$

(1) Prove that there exist constants $A, B$ ($0 < A < 1$), such that whenever $x$ has at least two different prime factors, we have $f(x) < Ax + B$.

(2) Prove that there exists positive integer $Q$, such that for any $n \in \mathbb{N}^*$, $a_n < Q$.

## Response 1
$\displaystyle x=\prod_{i=1}^{t}p_{i}^{e_{i}},\qquad e_{i}=v_{p_{i}}(x)\;(p_{1}<\dots <p_{t}).$ Put $$h(p,e)=\sum_{0\le j\le e/2}p^{\,e-2j}= \frac{p^{e+2}-p^{\varepsilon}}{p^{2}-1},\qquad \varepsilon\equiv e\pmod 2 ,\;0\le\varepsilon\le1 , \tag{1}$$ so that $h(p,e)=p^{e}$ if $e$ is odd and $h(p,e)=p^{e}+O(p^{e-2})$ if $e$ is even. If the $1=p_{i}^{0}$ which occurs for every even $e_{i}$ is counted only once we have $$f(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{t}h(p_{i},e_{i})-\delta (x),\qquad 0\le \delta (x)\le t-1, \tag{2}$$ and by (1) $$1\le \frac{h(p,e)}{p^{e}}=\frac{1-p^{-e-2+\varepsilon}}{1-p^{-2}} < \frac{p^{2}}{p^{2}-1}\le\frac43 ,\tag{3}$$ the equality $h(p,e)=p^{e}$ holding when $e$ is odd; in particular $$h(p,e)\le \frac43p^{e},\quad h(p,e)=p^{e}\;(e\ {\rm odd}),\quad h(p,e)\le \frac98p^{e}\;(p\ge3),\quad h(p,e)\le \frac54p^{e}\;(p\ge2,\ e\ge2). \tag{4}$$

If $t=1$ then (2)–(3) give $f(p^{e})>p^{e}$ and $f(p^{e})\le \frac43p^{e}$. Let $t\ge2$ and put $y_{i}=p_{i}^{e_{i}}\;(1\le i\le t)$; $x=\prod y_{i}$ and $y_{i}\ge2$, $y_{i}\le x/2^{\,t-1}$; hence from (2)–(4) $$f(x)\le\sum_{i=1}^{t}\frac{p_{i}^{2}}{p_{i}^{2}-1}\,y_{i} \le\sum_{i=1}^{t}\frac{p_{i}^{2}}{p_{i}^{2}-1}\, \frac{x}{\prod_{j\ne i}y_{j}} \le x\sum_{i=1}^{t}\frac{p_{i}^{2}}{p_{i}^{2}-1}\, 2^{-(t-1)} . \tag{5}$$

If $t\ge3$ the right–hand side of (5) does not exceed $\frac43\cdot\frac{3}{4}x=x$; a sharper estimate is obtained by using the smallest possible distinct $y_{i}$'s. For $t=3$ we may take $y_{1}=2$, $y_{2}=3$, $y_{3}=5$; then (4) gives $$f(x)\le \frac43\cdot\frac{x}{15}+\frac98\cdot\frac{x}{10} +\frac{25}{24}\cdot\frac{x}{6} <\frac12x\qquad(x>30),\tag{6}$$ and for $t>3$ the factor $2^{-(t-1)}$ makes the right–hand side of (5) still smaller. Hence there is a constant $c_{1}<1$ such that $f(x)\le c_{1}x$ for every $x$ with at least three distinct prime divisors and $x$ large.

Let $t=2$, $x=p^{a}q^{b}\;(p<q)$. If $a$ and $b$ are odd, $h(p,a)=p^{a}$, $h(q,b)=q^{b}$ and from (2) $$\frac{f(x)}{x}= \frac1{q^{b}}+\frac1{p^{a}} \le\frac12+\frac13=\frac56\;(x\ge6),\tag{7}$$ with equality only for $x=2\cdot3$. If $a\ge2$ then, by (4), $h(p,a)\le\frac54p^{a}$; the same holds for $b$. Consequently $$\frac{f(x)}{x}\le\frac54\Bigl(\frac1{q^{b}}+\frac1{p^{a}}\Bigr) \le\frac54\Bigl(\frac1{3}+\frac1{4}\Bigr)=\frac{35}{48} <\frac34\qquad(p^{a}\ge4,\;q^{b}\ge3). \tag{8}$$

If $a=1<b$ (the case $b=1<a$ is symmetric) we have $h(p,1)=p$ and, by (4), $h(q,b)\le \frac98q^{b}$; hence $$\frac{f(x)}{x}\le\frac1{q^{b}}+\frac98\frac1p \le\frac1{9}+\frac9{16}= \frac{97}{144}<1\qquad (p=2,\;q=3,\;b\ge2), \tag{9}$$ and the bound is still smaller when $p\ge3$ or $q\ge5$. From (6)–(9) we obtain $$\sup\Bigl\{\frac{f(x)}{x}\;:\;x>1,\;\omega(x)\ge2\Bigr\} =\frac56<1, \tag{10}$$ $\omega(x)$ denoting the number of distinct prime divisors of $x$. Hence we can choose $A$ with $\frac56<A<1$ ($A:=\tfrac9{10}$ for instance) and put $B:=\max_{1<x\le N}\bigl(f(x)-Ax\bigr)^{+}+1$, $N:=\max\{6,\;2^{m+4}\}$, where $u^{+}=\max(u,0)$. The maximum is finite, and (10) gives $f(x)-Ax\to-\infty$ as $x\to\infty$ through integers with $\omega(x)\ge2$. Consequently $$f(x)<Ax+B\qquad(x>1,\ \omega(x)\ge2) \tag{11}$$ with $0<A<1$. For a prime power we have, by (3), $f(p^{e})\le \frac43p^{e}$; hence for every $x\ge1$ $$f(x)\le Cx,\qquad C:=\frac43. \tag{12}$$

Put $T_{0}:=\bigl\lceil\frac{B+m}{1-A}\bigr\rceil$. If $\omega(y)\ge2$ and $y>T_{0}$ then (11) yields $f(y)\le Ay+B< y-m$. For a fixed $d\ge1$ the Diophantine equation $|p^{a}-q^{b}|=d$ has only finitely many solutions; hence for every $m$ there are only finitely many blocks of $m+1$ consecutive integers $>1$ all of which are prime powers. Let $L=L(m)>\max\{T_{0},N_{0}\}$ be so large that no interval $[u,u+m]$ with $u>L$ consists entirely of prime powers.

Now fix $a_{1},\dots ,a_{m}>0$ and suppose the sequence $(a_{n})$ defined by $a_{n+1}= \max_{0\le d\le m} f(a_{n-d}+d)$ ($n>m$) is unbounded. Choose $n_{1}<n_{2}<\cdots$ with $M_{k}:=a_{n_{k}+1}>\max_{j\le n_{k}}a_{j}$, $M_{k}\to\infty$. For each $k$ let $d_{k}\in[0,m]$ attain the maximum and put $y_{k}:=a_{n_{k}-d_{k}}+d_{k}$, $M_{k}=f(y_{k})$. Then $y_{k}\ge C^{-1}M_{k}\to\infty$. If $\omega(y_{k})\ge2$ and $y_{k}>T_{0}$ we would have $M_{k}=f(y_{k})<y_{k}-m\le a_{j_{k}}$, contradicting maximality. Hence for all large $k$, $y_{k}=p_{k}^{\,e_{k}}$ is a prime power $>L$. Applying the same reasoning iteratively, we obtain for each $k$ a chain of indices with each $y_{k}^{(i)}$ a prime power $>L$, and consecutive differences bounded by $O(1)$. This forces $m+1$ consecutive integers $>L$ to all be prime powers, contradicting the choice of $L$. Hence the sequence is bounded: there is $Q=Q(m,a_{1},\dots ,a_{m})$ such that $a_{n}<Q$ for every $n$. ∎

## Response 2
$$x=\prod_{i=1}^{k}p_{i}^{\,e_{i}}\qquad (e_{i}=v_{p_{i}}(x)),\qquad k=\omega (x)\ge 2 , \qquad u_{i}=p_{i}^{e_{i}},\; x=\prod_{i=1}^{k}u_{i},\;u_{i}\ge 2 .$$

Put $$g_{p}(e)=\sum_{\substack{0\le \alpha\le e\\ \alpha\equiv e\;(2)}}p^{\alpha} =\begin{cases} 1+p^{2}+\dots +p^{e}= \dfrac{p^{e+2}-1}{p^{2}-1}, & e=2t,\\ p+p^{3}+\dots +p^{e}=p\,\dfrac{p^{e+1}-1}{p^{2}-1}, & e=2t+1 , \end{cases}$$

hence $$g_{p}(e)<\frac{p^{2}}{p^{2}-1}\,p^{e}=c_{p}p^{e},\qquad c_{p}=1+\frac1{p^{2}-1}\le c_{2}= \frac43 ,\qquad c_{p}\downarrow\;(p\ge 3),\tag{1}$$

and, the value $1$ occurring in at most one $g_{p_{i}}(e_{i})$, $$f(x)=\sum_{p^{\alpha}\in S_{x}}p^{\alpha}\le \sum_{i=1}^{k}g_{p_{i}}(e_{i})\le\sum_{i=1}^{k}c_{p_{i}}u_{i} \le \frac43\sum_{i=1}^{k}u_{i}\;:=\,F(x) .\tag{2}$$

We estimate $\sum u_{i}$. Write $x=AB$ with $1<A\le B$, $\gcd (A,B)=1$, $A$ the product of some, $B$ the product of the remaining $u_{i}$'s. Because $k\ge 2$ we can take $A=u_{i_{0}}=\min u_{i}$; then $B=x/A\ge 2$ and $A\ge 2$. If $k=2$ we have $x=uv\;(u<v)$ and, with $u\ge2$, $$u+v=u+\frac{x}{u}\le 2+\frac{x}{2}\qquad(2\le u\le\sqrt x),\tag{3}$$

and from (2) and (1) $$f(x)\le F(x)\le c_{u}u+c_{v}v\le\frac43\Bigl(\frac{x}{2}+2\Bigr) =\frac23x+\frac83\qquad(k=2). \tag{4}$$

If $k\ge 3$ then $\prod_{j\ne i}u_{j}\ge 2^{\,k-1}\ge4$, whence $u_{i}\le x/4\;(1\le i\le k)$ and $$\sum_{i=1}^{k}u_{i}\le \frac{k}{2^{\,k-1}}\,x\le\frac34x \qquad(k\ge3) .\tag{5}$$

If $2\nmid x$ then $c_{p_{i}}\le c_{3}=9/8$ and (2),(5) give $F(x)\le\frac98\cdot\frac34x=\frac{27}{32}x$. If $2\mid x$, say $u_{1}=2^{e_{1}}$, then $k\ge3$ implies $\prod_{i\ge2}u_{i}\ge3^{\,k-1}\ge9$, so $u_{1}\le x/9$ and, for $i\ge2$, $u_{i}\le x/(2\cdot3^{\,k-2})\le x/6$; hence $\sum u_{i}\le x/9+2\cdot x/6=4x/9$ and, by (2), $$F(x)\le\frac43\cdot\frac49x=\frac{16}{27}x\qquad(2\mid x,\;k\ge3).\tag{6}$$

Consequently, with (4)–(6), $$f(x)\le F(x)\le A_{0}x+B_{0}\qquad\text{whenever }\omega(x)\ge2, \tag{7}$$ where we may take $A_{0}= \max\bigl\{\frac23,\frac{27}{32},\frac{16}{27}\bigr\}= \frac23<1$, $B_{0}=3$. Hence there are numbers $0<A<1$, $B>0$ (e.g. $A=5/6$, $B=10$) such that $\omega(x)\ge2 \Longrightarrow f(x)<Ax+B$ and (1) gives for every $y>1$ $f(y)\le c_{p_{\max }}\,y\le C y$, $C:=\frac43$.

Moreover, if $y=p^{e}\;(e\ge2)$ and $y>16$ then $f(y)$ is not a prime-power; indeed for $p=2$, $e\ge4$, $f(2^{e})=\frac{2^{e+2}-1}{3}\equiv0\pmod 3$, and for odd $p$, $e\ge2$, $f(p^{e})=\frac{p^{e+2}-\varepsilon}{p^{2}-1}$ is divisible by a prime $q\neq p$; in particular $\omega(f(p^{e}))\ge2$ for $p^{e}>9$. For a prime $p$ we have $f(p)=p$. Hence, with (8)–(9), $$y=p^{e}>16,\ e\ge2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f(f(y))\le A\,f(y)+B\le AC\,y+B=:K y+B,\tag{10}$$ and, because $A<3/4$, we can choose the $A$ so that $K=AC<1$.

Fix $m$ and choose $T_{0}>16$ so large that $At+B<t$ and $Kt+B<t$ for every $t\ge T_{0}$. Put $M:=\max_{1\le i\le m}a_{i}$, $Q_{0}:=\max \{M,T_{0}+m\}$, $Q:=C\,(Q_{0}+m)+1$.

We show $a_{n}<Q$ for every $n$. Suppose not and let $N>m$ be the smallest index with $a_{N}\ge Q$. Then $a_{N}= \max_{0\le r\le m} f(y_{r})$ with $y_{r}:=a_{N-1-r}+r\;(0\le r\le m)$, and $y_{r}\le Q_{0}+m$ for all $r$. Choose $r_{0}$ attaining the max and put $z:=y_{r_{0}}$; thus $a_{N}=f(z)$ and $z\le Q_{0}+m$.

If $\omega(z)\ge2$ and $z\ge T_{0}$ then $a_{N}=f(z)\le Az+B<z\le Q_{0}+m<Q$, impossible. If $\omega(z)\ge2$ and $z<T_{0}$ then $a_{N}\le AT_{0}+B<Q$, impossible. Hence $\omega(z)=1$; write $z=p^{e}$. If $e=1$ then $a_{N}=f(z)=z\le Q_{0}+m<Q$, contradiction; thus $e\ge2$ and $z>16$. By the remark above, $w:=a_{N}=f(z)$ satisfies $\omega(w)\ge2$ and, by (9), $w\le Cz\le C\,(Q_{0}+m)<Q$.

Consequently $a_{N}<Q$, contradicting the choice of $N$. Hence no index $N$ with $a_{N}\ge Q$ exists. Thus $a_{n}<Q$ for every $n\in\mathbb N^{*}$, where $Q$ depends only on $m$ and $a_{1},\dots ,a_{m}$ (for instance $A=2/3$, $B=3$, $C=4/3$, $K=8/9$ and $Q= \max\{M,100\}+m$ works). The proof is complete. ∎
