## Prompt
10. Let $S$ be a set of size 11. A random 12-tuple $\left(s_{1}, s_{2}, \ldots, s_{12}\right)$ of elements of $S$ is chosen uniformly at random. Moreover, let $\pi: S \rightarrow S$ be a permutation of $S$ chosen uniformly at random. The probability that $s_{i+1} \neq \pi\left(s_{i}\right)$ for all $1 \leq i \leq 12$ (where $s_{13}=s_{1}$ ) can be written as $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime positive integers. Compute $a$.

## Response 1
For a fixed permutation $\pi$ let  

\[
A_{uv}= 
\begin{cases}
1 & v\neq \pi(u),\\[2pt]
0 & v=\pi(u),
\end{cases}\qquad 
A=J_{11}-P ,
\]

where $J_{11}$ is the $11\times11$ all-ones matrix and $P$ is the matrix of
$\pi$.
The number of admissible $12$-tuples for this $\pi$ is the number of closed
walks of length $12$ in the digraph with adjacency matrix $A$, namely  

\[
N(\pi)=\operatorname{tr}A^{12}.
\]

Because $PJ=JP=J$, $J$ and $P$ are simultaneously diagonalizable.
$J$ has eigenvalues $11$ (once) and $0$ (ten times); $P$ has the
eigenvalues $\lambda$ of $\pi$, one of which is $1$ on the vector
$(1,\dots ,1)$.  Hence

\[
\operatorname{spec}(A)=\Bigl\{\,10,\;-\lambda\;(\lambda\neq1)\Bigr\},
\qquad 
\operatorname{tr}A^{12}=10^{12}+
\sum_{\lambda\neq1}\lambda^{12}
=10^{12}+\operatorname{tr}P^{12}-1 .
\]

$\operatorname{tr}P^{12}$ is the number $f(\pi)$ of points fixed by
$\pi^{12}$, i.e. the number of points lying on cycles of $\pi$ whose
lengths divide $12$. Hence  

\[
N(\pi)=10^{12}+f(\pi)-1 .
\]

Summation over the $11!$ permutations gives the total number of admissible
pairs $(s,\pi)$.  For a fixed point $x\in S$ the number of permutations in
which the cycle containing $x$ has a prescribed length $\ell\le11$ is
$(11-1)!=10!$; it is admissible iff $\ell\mid12$.
The divisors of $12$ not exceeding $11$ are $1,2,3,4,6$, five of them, so

\[
\sum_{\pi\in\mathfrak S_{11}}f(\pi)=
11\cdot5\cdot10!=55\cdot10!.
\]

Consequently

\[
\begin{aligned}
\#\{(s,\pi):s_{i+1}\neq\pi(s_i)\}
&=\sum_{\pi}N(\pi)
 =11!\,(10^{12}-1)+55\cdot10! \\
&=10!\bigl[11\cdot10^{12}+44\bigr]
 =11!\,(10^{12}+4).
\end{aligned}
\]

Since there are $11!\,11^{12}$ pairs in all, the required probability is

\[
\Pr=\frac{11!\,(10^{12}+4)}{11!\,11^{12}}
     =\frac{10^{12}+4}{11^{12}},
\]

which is already in lowest terms because $10^{12}+4\equiv5\pmod{11}$.
In particular the reduced numerator is

\[
10^{12}+4=1\,000\,000\,000\,004.
\]

Thus, with $\displaystyle \Pr=\frac{a}{b}$ in lowest terms,
$a=10^{12}+4$.

## Response 2
\[
n=|S|=11,\qquad L=12 ,\qquad A=J-P ,\qquad P=\text{perm-matrix of }\pi .
\]

For a fixed permutation \(\pi\) let  

\[
A_{ij}= \begin{cases}1 & j\neq \pi(i)\\ 0& j=\pi(i)\end{cases}=J-P .
\]

The number of admissible 12‑tuples is the number of closed walks of length \(L\) in the digraph with adjacency matrix \(A\):
\[
N(\pi)=\operatorname{Tr}\!\bigl(A^{L}\bigr)=\operatorname{Tr}\!\bigl((J-P)^{L}\bigr).
\]

Since \(JP=PJ=J\), \(J\) and \(P\) commute.  
The spectrum of \(P\) consists of the eigenvalues \(\lambda\) of the permutation \(\pi\); the all‑ones vector is an eigenvector of \(P\) with eigenvalue \(1\) and of \(J\) with eigenvalue \(n\).  
All other common eigenvectors satisfy \(Jv=0\).

Hence the eigenvalues of \(J-P\) are  

* \(n-1\) (once, from the all‑ones vector), and  
* \(-\lambda\) for the remaining \(n-1\) eigenvalues \(\lambda\) of \(P\) (the eigenvalue \(\lambda=1\) coming from the all‑ones vector is omitted).

Therefore
\[
\operatorname{Tr}\bigl((J-P)^{L}\bigr)= (n-1)^{L}+\sum_{\lambda\neq1}(-\lambda)^{L}
        =(n-1)^{L}+ \sum_{\text{all }\lambda}(-\lambda)^{L}-(-1)^{L}.
\]

Because \(\sum_{\lambda}\lambda^{L}= \operatorname{Tr}(P^{L}) =\#\{a\in S:\pi^{L}(a)=a\}=:f_{L}(\pi)\),

\[
N(\pi)= (n-1)^{L}+(-1)^{L}\bigl(f_{L}(\pi)-1\bigr).
\]

With \(L=12\) (even), \(n=11\),

\[
N(\pi)=10^{12}+f_{12}(\pi)-1.
\]

Summing over all permutations,
\[
\begin{aligned}
N_{\text{tot}} &=\sum_{\pi\in S_{11}} N(\pi)
                =11!\,10^{12}+ \sum_{\pi}f_{12}(\pi)-11! .
\end{aligned}
\]

For a fixed \(a\in S\), \(\pi^{12}(a)=a\) iff the cycle of \(\pi\) containing \(a\) has length \(d\mid12\).  
The number of permutations in which a given point lies in a prescribed cycle of length \(d\le11\) is

\[
\binom{10}{d-1}(d-1)!\,(11-d)! =10!.
\]

The divisors of 12 not exceeding 11 are \(1,2,3,4,6\) – five values. Hence

\[
\sum_{\pi} f_{12}(\pi)=11\cdot 5\cdot 10! =5\cdot 11!.
\]

Consequently
\[
N_{\text{tot}} = 11!\bigl(10^{12}-1+5\bigr)=11!\,(10^{12}+4).
\]

The total number of possible \((\mathbf s,\pi)\) pairs is \(11^{12}\cdot 11!\). Thus

\[
\Pr=\frac{N_{\text{tot}}}{11^{12}\,11!}= \frac{10^{12}+4}{11^{12}}.
\]

Since \(10\equiv-1\pmod{11}\), \(10^{12}+4\equiv1+4\equiv5\pmod{11}\); the fraction is in lowest terms, so

\[
a = 10^{12}+4 = 1\,000\,000\,000\,004.
\]

\[
1000000000004
\]
