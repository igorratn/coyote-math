## Prompt
A group consist of $n$ tourists. Among every $3$ of them there are $2$ which are not familiar. For every partition of the tourists in $2$ buses you can find $2$ tourists that are in the same bus and are familiar with each other. Prove that is a tourist familiar to at most $\frac{2n}{5}$ tourists.

## Response 1
Let $G$ be the graph whose vertices are the tourists and where $xy$ is an
edge iff $x$ and $y$ are familiar.  
"Among any three there are two who do not know each other'' says that $G$
contains no triangle; the second hypothesis says that no set of vertices
can be split into two independent sets, i.e. $G$ is not bipartite.
Hence $G$ contains an odd circuit; because it is triangle–free the
shortest odd circuit has length
$$
\begin{equation*}
C=v_{0}v_{1}\dots v_{2k}v_{0},\qquad |C|=m=2k+1\ge 5,
\tag{1}
\end{equation*}
$$
and $C$ is chordless: a chord would join two vertices of $C$ and,
together with one of the two arcs of $C$, would give an odd circuit
shorter than $C$.

Denote by $N_{C}(x)=N(x)\cap V(C)$ the neighbours of a vertex $x\notin C$
on $C$.

Lemma.  For $x\notin C$, $|N_{C}(x)|\le2$; if $|N_{C}(x)|=2$ the two
neighbours are at distance $2$ on $C$, and consequently $C$ is an induced
$C_{5}$ when the bound is attained for many vertices.

Indeed, let $x$ be joined to $v_{i}$ and $v_{j}\;(i\neq j)$ and let
$d\;(2\le d\le k)$ be the length of the shorter $v_{i}\!-\!v_{j}$ path on
$C$.  The two circuits
$$
v_{i}x v_{j}P_{ij},\qquad v_{i}x v_{j}Q_{ij},
$$
where $P_{ij}$ and $Q_{ij}$ are the two $v_{i}\!-\!v_{j}$ arcs of $C$,
have lengths $d+2$ and $m-d+2$; their sum is $m+4$, so the parities are
different.  If $d$ is even, $d+2$ is even and $m-d+2$ is odd; minimality
of $m$ gives
$$
m-d+2\ge m,\qquad\text{i.e. }d\le2 .
\tag{2}
$$
If $d$ is odd, $d+2$ is odd and $d+2\ge m$, whence $d\ge m-2>k$, impossible.
Hence a pair of distinct neighbours of $x$ on $C$ can occur only for
$d=2$; for $d=1$ we would have the triangle $xv_{i}v_{i+1}$.  Thus two
neighbours are non-consecutive and at distance $2$.

If $x$ had three distinct neighbours on $C$, two of them would be at
distance $\ge3$ on $C$; for $m\ge7$ that distance is $\ge3$, and the
foregoing argument gives an odd circuit of length $\le m-2$, contrary to
the choice of $C$.  For $m=5$ three neighbours would contain a consecutive
pair and would create a triangle.  Hence $|N_{C}(x)|\le2$ for every
$x\notin C$, and when equality holds the two neighbours are the two
vertices $v_{i-1},v_{i+1}$ ($i$ mod $m$); in particular $x$ is adjacent to
no other $v_{j}$.

Consequently
$$
\begin{equation*}
e\bigl(C,V(G)\setminus C\bigr)=\sum_{x\notin C}|N_{C}(x)|
      \le 2\,(n-m). \tag{3}
\end{equation*}
$$

Suppose, to obtain a contradiction, that
$$
\begin{equation*}
d(v)>\frac{2n}{5}\qquad\text{for every }v\in V(G). \tag{4}
\end{equation*}
$$
Summation of the degrees of the vertices of $C$ gives, because each
$v_{i}$ has exactly the two neighbours $v_{i-1},v_{i+1}$ on $C$,
$$
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}d(v_{i})
      =2m+e\bigl(C,V\setminus C\bigr)
      \stackrel{(3)}{\le}2m+2(n-m)=2n . \tag{5}
$$
From (4),
$$
\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}d(v_{i})>m\cdot\frac{2n}{5}= \frac{2(2k+1)}{5}\,n .
\tag{6}
$$
Hence $\dfrac{2(2k+1)}{5}n<2n$, i.e. $2k+1<5$, which is impossible
because $2k+1\ge5$.  If $2k+1\ge7$ then $\dfrac{2(2k+1)}{5}\ge\frac{14}{5}>2$
and (5)–(6) are incompatible; thus (4) cannot hold when $m\ge7$.

Therefore the shortest odd circuit must have length $5$; put $m=5$.
For $m=5$, (5) is $\sum_{i=0}^{4}d(v_{i})\le2n$, whereas (6) is
$\sum_{i=0}^{4}d(v_{i})>5\cdot 2n/5 =2n$, a strict contradiction.
Hence (4) is false: $G$ cannot have minimum degree $>2n/5$.

It follows that $G$ possesses a vertex $v_{0}$ with
$$
d(v_{0})\le \frac{2n}{5},
$$
i.e. a tourist who knows at most $2n/5$ of the others.  If all degrees
were $>2n/5$, $G$ would be bipartite, so for every partition of the
tourists into two buses each bus would be an independent set, contrary to
the hypothesis.  Hence some tourist is familiar with at most $\lfloor
2n/5
floor\le 2n/5$ other tourists.

For $5\mid n$ the bound is best possible: partition the $n$ tourists into
five classes $A_{0},\dots ,A_{4}$ of $n/5$ each, join every vertex of
$A_{i}$ to all vertices of $A_{i+1}$ and $A_{i-1}$ (indices mod 5) and
put no other edges.  The obtained graph is triangle-free, its odd
girth is $5$, it is not bipartite, and each vertex has exactly $2n/5$
neighbours.  Thus $2n/5$ cannot be improved.  ∎

## Response 2
$\Gamma $ be the graph whose vertices are the tourists and join $x,y$
by an edge iff $x$ and $y$ are familiar; *familiar* is symmetric, so $\Gamma $
is simple.  

(1) "Among any three tourists two are not familiar'' means that no three
pairwise adjacent vertices occur, i.e. $\Gamma $ contains no $K_{3}$;  
$\Gamma $ is triangle‑free.

(2) Colour the vertices by the bus in which the tourist travels.
If a 2-colouring with no monochromatic edge existed, that partition would
have no two familiar tourists in the same bus, contrary to the hypothesis.
Hence $\chi (\Gamma )\ge 3$; $\Gamma $ is not bipartite and contains an odd
cycle.  Let  

$$
C=v_{1}v_{2}\dots v_{2k+1}v_{1}\qquad(k\ge 2)
\tag{1}
$$

be a shortest odd cycle; hence $C$ is chordless and $2k+1$ is the odd girth.
Put $m=|V\Gamma|=n$.

Suppose, to obtain a contradiction, that  

$$
d(v)>\frac{2m}{5}\qquad(v\in V\Gamma) \tag{2}
$$

and denote $A_{i}=N(v_{i})\setminus V(C)$ $(1\le i\le 2k+1)$.
Because $\Gamma $ is $K_{3}$-free,
$$
N(v_{i})\cap N(v_{i+1})=\varnothing ,\qquad A_{i}\cap A_{i+1}=\varnothing .
\tag{3}
$$

Lemma.  For $x\notin V(C)$ let $t(x)=|N(x)\cap V(C)|$.
Then $t(x)\le 2$, and if $t(x)=2$ the two neighbours of $x$ on $C$ are
consecutive vertices at distance $2$ on $C$.

Indeed, let $x$ be joined to $v_{p},v_{q}\;(p<q)$ and let $d=q-p$
$(1\le d\le k)$ be the length of the shorter $v_{p}$–$v_{q}$ arc of $C$.
The cycle $xv_{p}\ldots v_{q}x$ has length $d+2$.
If $d$ is odd then $d+2$ is odd and, because $d\le k$,  
$d+2\le k+2<2k+1$ for $k\ge 2$; this would be an odd cycle shorter than
$C$, impossible.  Hence $d$ is even; in particular $d\neq1$, so $x$ is not
joined to two consecutive vertices of $C$.

If $d\ge4$ then the complementary arc $v_{q}\ldots v_{p}$ has length
$2k+1-d$, which is odd and $2k+1-d+2=2k+3-d<2k+1$; again a shorter odd
cycle occurs.  Thus $d=2$. Consequently two distinct neighbours of $x$ on
$C$ are at distance $2$, and three distinct neighbours would contain two
with odd distance, which is impossible; hence $t(x)\le2$, and the
assertion follows.

Hence every vertex outside $C$ is joined to at most two vertices of $C$, so

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{2k+1}|A_{i}|=
\sum_{v\in V(C)}\bigl(d(v)-2\bigr)=
\sum_{x\notin C}t(x)\le 2\,(m-2k-1). \tag{4}
$$

By (2), $d(v_{i})-2>\dfrac{2m}{5}-2$, whence from (4)

$$
(2k+1)\Bigl(\frac{2m}{5}-2\Bigr)
   <\sum_{i}|A_{i}|\le 2m-4k-2 .
$$

Cancelling $-4k-2$ we obtain

$$
\frac{2m}{5}(2k+1)<2m\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad 4k+2<10,
$$

i.e. $k<2$, in contradiction with $k\ge2$ (the graph is $K_{3}$-free, so
the odd girth is at least $5$).  Thus (2) is impossible.

Hence $\Gamma $ possesses a vertex $v_{0}$ with

$$
d(v_{0})\le\frac{2m}{5}\, .
$$

If $d(v_{0})=\frac{2m}{5}$ equality holds in the preceding computation;
then $k=2$, $|C|=5$ and $t(x)=2$ for every $x\notin C$, the two neighbours
being at distance $2$ on $C$.  Writing $V_{i}= \{v_{i}\}\cup
\bigl(N(v_{i-1})\cap N(v_{i+1})\bigr)$, the sets $V_{1},\dots ,V_{5}$ are
pairwise disjoint independent sets, $V(\Gamma)=\bigcup V_{i}$, and two
different $V_{i},V_{j}$ are completely joined iff $j\equiv i\pm1\pmod5$.
Thus $\Gamma $ is a blow-up of the $5$-cycle; each vertex is adjacent
precisely to the two neighbouring classes, whence
$d(v)=|V_{i-1}|+|V_{i+1}|$.  In particular the smallest class has
$|V_{i}|\le m/5$, and a vertex of that class has
$d(v)\le 2m/5$, the bound being attained for the balanced blow-up
$|V_{i}|=m/5$ ($5\mid m$), i.e. for the $5$-cycle itself when $m=5$.

Consequently some tourist knows at most $2n/5$ of the other tourists.
If every tourist knew more than $2n/5$ others, (2) would hold and $\Gamma $
would be bipartite, i.e. the tourists could be split into the two buses
with no familiar pair sharing a bus, contrary to hypothesis. ∎
