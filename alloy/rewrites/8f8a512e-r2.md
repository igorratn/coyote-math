The positions of the two men may be taken on the integer line.
Put the origin at $B$'s first position and let the (signed) distance
$x_{A}-x_{B}$ at the beginning of a move of $A$ be $s$ $(s\neq 0)$.
When $A$ moves he changes the distance to

$$
s_{1}=s\pm a ,\qquad
\begin{cases}
s_{1}=0 &\hbox{(A moves on B) } \\
|s_{1}|=b &\hbox{(B catches A at his next move)}
\end{cases}
\tag{1}
$$

are the two ways in which $A$ loses at once.

If $|s_{1}|\neq b$ and $s_{1}\neq0$ the turn passes to $B$; he changes the
distance to

$$
s' = s_{1}\mp b =s\pm a\mp b\in\{s\pm (b-a),s\pm (b+a)\}
\tag{2}
$$

and the game goes on with the new starting distance $s'$.

Hence after every complete round the residue of the distance modulo
$b$ is changed only by $A$:

$$
s'\equiv s\pm a\pmod b .
\tag{3}
$$

Put $d=\gcd (a,b)$, $a=d a_{1}$, $b=d b_{1}$, $(a_{1},b_{1})=1$ and write
$s=d\sigma$, $L=d\lambda$ $({\lambda\in\mathbb Z})$.
Dividing (3) by $d$,

$$
\sigma'\equiv\sigma\pm a_{1}\pmod{b_{1}} .
\tag{4}
$$

Thus $A$ moves the point $\sigma$ on the cyclic graph
$\mathbf Z_{b_{1}}$ by the steps $\pm a_{1}$.
$B$ wins at his move iff after $A$'s move the residue is
$0\pmod{b_{1}}$ **and** the absolute value of the distance after that
move is exactly $b$ $(=d b_{1})$.

Consequently $A$ can avoid being caught forever precisely when he can keep
the residue $\sigma$ (mod $b_{1}$) away from $0$ forever.
On the graph $\mathbf Z_{b_{1}}$ the edges join
$i$ to $i\pm a_{1}$ (mod $b_{1}$); because $(a_{1},b_{1})=1$ the
graph is the ordinary $b_{1}$-cycle.
Removing the vertex $0$ leaves the path
$a_{1},2a_{1},\ldots ,(b_{1}-1)a_{1}$ (indices mod $b_{1}$).
On this path $A$ can walk indefinitely, changing direction at the two
endâ€“vertices, **unless the path has no edge**.

The path has no edge only when $b_{1}=2$; then $a_{1}=1$.

Hence

- if $(a_{1},b_{1})\neq(1,2)$ (i.e. $b\neq2a$) the vertices distinct
from $0$ form a path with at least one edge.

Starting from the residue $\lambda$ (mod $b_{1}$) (if $\lambda\equiv0$
the first move of $A$ makes it non-zero, because $a_{1}\neq0$) $A$
can forever move on that path, never making the residue $0$ after his
move; therefore $|s_{1}|$ is never $b$ and $B$ never catches $A$.

- if $b_{1}=2$ then $a_{1}=1$; hence $b=2d$ and $a=d$, i.e.

$$
b=2a .
\tag{5}
$$

The graph is $0$-$1$-$0$; after deleting $0$ one isolated vertex
remains.  Consequently $A$ cannot make a move without putting the
residue equal to $0\pmod2$.  Let the initial (signed) distance be
$s$ with $d\mid s$; otherwise (2) shows that $s$ stays prime to
$d$ and can never become $0$, so $B$ can never catch $A$.
Assume then that

$$
a\mid L \qquad(L=d\lambda) . 
\tag{6}
$$

If the beginning distance is $s=da$ (mod $2a$) the two moves
$s\pm a$ are $0$ ($A$ meets $B$) and $2a=b$ ($B$ catches $A$); $A$ is lost.
If the beginning distance is $s=2ka$ $(k\ge1)$ the move
$s-a=(2k-1)a$ is safe; $B$ answers with the move
$-b$ and puts the distance at $(2k-1)a$.  At the next round
$A$ is at a distance $(2k-1)a$; his two possible moves are
$2ka$ (which returns the position) and $(2k-2)a$.  By always moving
towards $A$, $B$ reduces $|k|$ by one every two rounds, and after
finitely many rounds the distance becomes $a$; $A$ is then forced to lose.
Thus under (5)â€“(6) $B$ catches $A$ after at most $\lceil L/a\rceil$
moves: his winning strategy is always to step towards $A$.

Consequently $B$ possesses a (finite) forced win **iff** the two
numbers are in the ratio $1:2$ and the initial separation is a multiple
of the slower step.

In the original notation $(d=\gcd(a,b))$:

$B$ wins $\iff b=2\gcd(a,b)$ and $\gcd(a,b)\mid L$; because
$a<b$ this is simply

$b=2a$ and $a\mid L$.

If $b\neq2a$ or $a\nmid L$, $A$ makes the distance after each of
his moves congruent to $a\pmod b$ (congruent to $\pm a_{1}\pmod{b_{1}}$)
and shuttles between the two neighbours of the residue $0$ on the
$b_{1}$-cycle; the distance never becomes $b$ and $A$ never steps on
$B$.  The game goes on forever, so $B$ has no winning strategy. âˆŽ
