
For $n \ge 1$, define the Hermite function of the second kind as:

$$Q_n(z) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{-t^2} H_n(t)}{z-t} dt.$$

Claim:

$$\lim_{z\to\infty} z^{n+1}Q_n(z) = \sqrt{\pi} n!.$$

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

Conclusion: True.

In the Hermite case $(a,b)=(-\infty,\infty)$, the condition $z\notin[a,b]$ means $z\in\mathbb C\setminus\mathbb R$. Set $\rho(w)=e^{-w^2}$ and $y_n(t)=H_n(t)$. Using the Nikiforov–Uvarov definition

$$Q_n^{NU}(z)=\frac{1}{\rho(z)}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{\rho(t)y_n(t)}{t-z}dt, \qquad z\in\mathbb C\setminus\mathbb R,$$

Nikiforov and Uvarov (Special Functions of Mathematical Physics, Birkhäuser, 1988, Chapter II, §11, Eqs. 4 and 7) prove that

$$Q_n^{NU}(z)\sim-\frac{d_n^2}{a_n\rho(z)z^{n+1}}\left(1+O(z^{-1})\right), \quad z\to\infty, \ z\in\mathbb C\setminus\mathbb R,$$

where $a_n$ is the leading coefficient of $y_n$ and $d_n^2=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\rho(t)y_n(t)^2dt$.

For $n\ge1$ and $z\in\mathbb C\setminus\mathbb R$, define

$$Q_n(z)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-t^2}H_n(t)}{z-t}dt.$$

Since $1/(z-t)=-1/(t-z)$, one has

$$Q_n(z)=-\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{\rho(t)y_n(t)}{t-z}dt=-\rho(z)Q_n^{NU}(z).$$

Multiplying the cited asymptotic by $-\rho(z)$ gives

$$Q_n(z)\sim\frac{d_n^2}{a_n}z^{-(n+1)}\left(1+O(z^{-1})\right),$$

hence $\lim_{z\to\infty}z^{n+1}Q_n(z)=d_n^2/a_n$. For physicists’ Hermite polynomials, $a_n=2^n$ and $d_n^2=\sqrt{\pi}2^n n!$. Therefore

$$\lim_{z\to\infty}z^{n+1}Q_n(z)=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}2^n n!}{2^n}=\sqrt{\pi}n!,$$

which matches the claim.
