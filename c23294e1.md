For $\nu > -1$, let $0 < j_{\nu,1} < j_{\nu,2} < \cdots$ denote the positive zeros of the Bessel function of the first kind $J_\nu(x)$. The Rayleigh sums (or spectral zeta functions of the Bessel operator) are defined by

$$\sigma_s(\nu) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{j_{\nu,k}^{2s}}, \qquad s = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

It is classically known that these sums can be expressed as rational functions of $\nu$. The first three are:

$$\sigma_1(\nu) = \frac{1}{4(\nu+1)},$$

$$\sigma_2(\nu) = \frac{1}{16(\nu+1)^2(\nu+2)},$$

$$\sigma_3(\nu) = \frac{1}{32(\nu+1)^3(\nu+2)(\nu+3)}.$$

By examining the structure of these formulas, one observes a pattern: the denominator of $\sigma_s(\nu)$ is $2^{2s-1+\lfloor s/2 \rfloor}$ times $\prod_{j=1}^{s}(\nu+j)$ with $(\nu+1)$ raised to the $s$-th power, and the numerator is $1$.

Claim: This pattern continues to $s = 4$. Specifically,

$$\sigma_4(\nu) = \frac{1}{64(\nu+1)^4(\nu+2)(\nu+3)(\nu+4)}.$$

Determine whether this claim is True or False, and give a rigorous proof of your conclusion.

The claim is False.

The method below follows the classical treatment of Rayleigh sums via the Hadamard product for $J_\nu$. See G. N. Watson, *A Treatise on the Theory of Bessel Functions*, 2nd ed., Cambridge University Press, 1944, §15.51.

Using the Hadamard product
$$J_\nu(x)=\frac{(x/2)^\nu}{\Gamma(\nu+1)}\prod_{k=1}^\infty\left(1-\frac{x^2}{j_{\nu,k}^2}\right),$$
set
$$f(t)=\sum_{m=0}^\infty a_m t^m,\qquad a_m=\frac{(-1)^m}{m!(\nu+1)_m},\qquad t=\frac{x^2}{4}.$$
Then
$$\log f(t)=-\sum_{s=1}^\infty \frac{4^s\sigma_s(\nu)}{s}t^s,$$
so coefficient comparison gives
$$na_n+\sum_{j=1}^n 4^j\sigma_j(\nu)a_{n-j}=0 \qquad(n\ge1).$$

Using
$$a_1=-\frac{1}{\nu+1},\quad a_2=\frac{1}{2(\nu+1)(\nu+2)},\quad a_3=-\frac{1}{6(\nu+1)(\nu+2)(\nu+3)},\quad a_4=\frac{1}{24(\nu+1)(\nu+2)(\nu+3)(\nu+4)},$$
together with
$$\sigma_1(\nu)=\frac{1}{4(\nu+1)},\quad \sigma_2(\nu)=\frac{1}{16(\nu+1)^2(\nu+2)},\quad \sigma_3(\nu)=\frac{1}{32(\nu+1)^3(\nu+2)(\nu+3)},$$
the $n=4$ relation yields
$$\sigma_4(\nu)=\frac{5\nu+11}{256(\nu+1)^4(\nu+2)^2(\nu+3)(\nu+4)}.$$

Therefore
$$\sigma_4(\nu)\neq \frac{1}{64(\nu+1)^4(\nu+2)(\nu+3)(\nu+4)},$$
so the claim is False.
