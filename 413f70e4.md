Let $P_\ell^m(x)$ denote the associated Legendre functions on $x \in [-1,1]$ for integers $\ell \geq m \geq 0$, and define

$$\Theta_{\ell m}(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{2} \frac{(\ell-m)!}{(\ell+m)!}} P_\ell^m(x),$$

the $L^2$-normalized polar components of the spherical harmonics. Let the first-order differential operator $\mathcal{L}$ act on functions $f$ by

$$(\mathcal{L} f)(x) = (1-x^2) \frac{df}{dx}(x).$$

Fix an integer $m \geq 1$ and a point $x_0 \in (-1,1)$, and define

$$S_N^{(m)}(x_0) = \sum_{\ell=m}^N \frac{1}{\ell^2} \left| (\mathcal{L} \Theta_{\ell m})(x_0) \right|^2.$$

Claim:

$$\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{S_N^{(m)}(x_0)}{N} = \frac{\sqrt{1-x_0^2}}{\pi}.$$

Determine, with rigorous proof, whether the claim is true or false.

Conclusion True.

Let $x_0 = \cos\theta_0$ with $\theta_0 \in (0, \pi)$ and note that the operator $\mathcal{L} = (1 - x^2) \frac{d}{dx} = -\sin\theta \frac{d}{d\theta}$. Because the final sum $S_N^{(m)}(x_0)$ involves the absolute square of the terms, the sign factor $(-1)^m$ is ignored from the beginning of the derivation.

For fixed $m \geq 1$ and fixed $\theta_0 \in (0, \pi)$, the interior Hilb asymptotic for associated Legendre functions provides the following approximation:

$$P_\ell^m(\cos\theta_0) \sim (-1)^m \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \ell^{m-\frac{1}{2}} (\sin\theta_0)^{-m-\frac{1}{2}} \cos\left( \left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right)\theta_0 - \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{m\pi}{2} \right) + O(\ell^{m-\frac{3}{2}}).$$

Using the asymptotic for the normalization constant:

$$\sqrt{\frac{2\ell + 1}{2} \frac{(\ell - m)!}{(\ell + m)!}} = \ell^{\frac{1}{2}-m} \bigl(1 + O(\ell^{-1})\bigr).$$

Multiplying these two asymptotics yields the normalized polar factor $\Theta_{\ell m}(\cos\theta_0)$:

$$\Theta_{\ell m}(\cos\theta_0) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} (\sin\theta_0)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cos\left( \left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right)\theta_0 - \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{m\pi}{2} \right) + O(\ell^{-1}).$$

Differentiation with respect to $\theta$ and application of the operator $\mathcal{L} = -\sin\theta \frac{d}{d\theta}$ leads to:

$$\mathcal{L} \Theta_{\ell m}(\cos\theta_0) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right) (\sin\theta_0)^{\frac{1}{2}} \sin\left( \left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right)\theta_0 - \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{m\pi}{2} \right) + O(1).$$

In calculating the summand, the $(\ell + 1/2)$ term scales as $\ell$ for large $\ell$, such that $(\ell + 1/2)^2/\ell^2 \to 1$:

$$\frac{1}{\ell^2} \bigl| \mathcal{L} \Theta_{\ell m}(x_0) \bigr|^2 = \frac{2}{\pi} (\sin\theta_0) \sin^2\left( \left(\ell + \frac{1}{2}\right)\theta_0 - \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{m\pi}{2} \right) + O(\ell^{-1}).$$

Summing from $\ell = m$ to $N$ and dividing by $N$, the $O(\ell^{-1})$ remainder contributes $o(1)$. The main term averages to $1/2$ because the sum $\sum e^{i(2\ell+1)\theta_0}$ is a bounded geometric series for $\theta_0 \in (0, \pi)$, which ensures the oscillatory component vanishes under the $1/N$ scaling:

$$\frac{1}{N} \sum_{\ell=m}^{N} \sin^2\bigl( (\ell + \tfrac{1}{2})\theta_0 + \alpha \bigr) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\ell=m}^{N} \left( \frac{1 - \cos( (2\ell + 1)\theta_0 + 2\alpha )}{2} \right) \to \frac{1}{2}.$$

Hence:

$$\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{S_N^{(m)}(x_0)}{N} = \frac{2}{\pi} \sin\theta_0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\sin\theta_0}{\pi} = \frac{\sqrt{1 - x_0^2}}{\pi}.$$

The claimed linear growth and the stated limit are correct.
