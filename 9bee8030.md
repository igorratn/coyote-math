
Let $Y_{\ell m}(\theta, \phi)$ denote the standard spherical harmonics on the unit sphere $\mathbb{S}^2$ with normalization

$$Y_{\ell m}(\theta, \phi) = \sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi}\frac{(\ell-m)!}{(\ell+m)!}} P_{\ell}^m(\cos\theta) e^{im\phi}$$

where $P_{\ell}^m$ are the associated Legendre functions and $(\theta, \phi) \in [0,\pi] \times [0,2\pi)$.

Consider a rotation of the sphere about the $y$-axis by angle $\beta \in (0, \pi)$. The rotated spherical harmonic is given by

$$\tilde{Y}_{\ell m}(\theta, \phi; \beta) = \sum_{m'=-\ell}^{\ell} d_{m'm}^{\ell}(\beta) Y_{\ell m'}(\theta, \phi)$$

where $d_{m'm}^{\ell}(\beta)$ are the reduced Wigner $d$-functions.

For integers $\ell \geq 1$ and $|m| \leq \ell$, define the pole evaluation

$$F_{\ell m}(\beta) = \lim_{\theta \to 0^+} \tilde{Y}_{\ell m}(\theta, 0; \beta)$$

Claim

For every fixed rotation angle $\beta \in (0, \pi)$ and for all $\ell \geq 1$ with $|m| \leq \ell$, the pole evaluation satisfies

$$F_{\ell m}(\beta) = \sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi}} P_{\ell}(\cos\beta) \cdot \delta_{m,0}$$

where $\delta_{m,0}$ is the Kronecker delta.

Determine with rigorous proof whether the claim is True or False.

Conclusion False.

At the north pole $\theta=0$, the associated Legendre functions satisfy $P_\ell^{m'}(1)=0$ for all $m'\neq0$. For $m'>0$ this follows directly from the factor $(1-x^2)^{m'/2}$ in the Rodrigues formula, while for $m'<0$ it follows from the identity:

$$P_\ell^{-m}(x)=(-1)^m\frac{(\ell-m)!}{(\ell+m)!}P_\ell^{m}(x)$$

For $m'=0$ one has $P_\ell^0(1)=P_\ell(1)=1$. Therefore

$$Y_{\ell m'}(0, \phi) = \sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi}} \delta_{m',0}$$

Substituting into the rotation formula gives

$$F_{\ell m}(\beta) = \sum_{m'=-\ell}^{\ell} d_{m'm}^{\ell}(\beta) Y_{\ell m'}(0, 0) = d_{0m}^{\ell}(\beta) \sqrt{\frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi}}$$

For some $m \neq 0$, the reduced Wigner $d$-function $d_{0m}^{\ell}(\beta)$ is nonzero. 

Taking $\ell=1$ and $m=1$ as a counterexample, the standard convention gives 

$$d^{1}_{01}(\beta)=-\frac{\sin\beta}{\sqrt2},$$

hence

$$F_{11}(\beta) = \sqrt{\frac{3}{4\pi}}\,d^{1}_{01}(\beta) = -\sqrt{\frac{3}{4\pi}}\frac{\sin\beta}{\sqrt2}.$$

For all $\beta\in(0,\pi)$,

$$F_{11}(\beta)\neq0.$$
This contradicts the claim, which requires $F_{11}(\beta) = 0$ by the factor $\delta_{1,0}$.
